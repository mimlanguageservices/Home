Platform Decline & Digital Power — A Technology English Lesson

Discussion: Platform Degradation

1. Platform Experience: Think about a social media platform or app you've used for several years. How has your experience changed? Have you noticed more advertisements, altered algorithms, or features designed to keep you engaged longer?

2. Business Model Impact: Why do you think platforms prioritise attracting users first, then businesses, before finally extracting maximum value? What does this pattern reveal about how digital businesses operate in competitive markets?

3. User Response: When platforms become frustrating or manipulative, what options do users have? Have you ever stopped using a platform because it became too difficult or annoying to use? What made you switch?

4. Regulation and Solutions: Should governments regulate how digital platforms operate? What role should competition, user education, or alternative platforms play in preventing the enshittification of digital services?

What is Enshittification?

My name is Alex, and I've been using social media and online platforms for over ten years. Recently, many of my favourite apps and websites have become much more difficult to use. Then I learned about something called enshittification.

This word was created by a technology writer named Cory Doctorow. He noticed that many digital platforms follow the same pattern: they start great, but then slowly become terrible.

For example, I remember when Facebook was simple and clean. I could see posts from friends in the order they were posted. Now, the algorithm decides what I see, and there are advertisements everywhere. The platform has become much more focused on keeping me engaged and making money than on helping me connect with friends.

The same thing happened with YouTube. When I first started using it, there were very few ads. Now there are multiple ad breaks in each video, and many creators struggle to earn money because the rules keep changing.

The term "enshittification" was coined by journalist and activist Cory Doctorow to describe the predictable decline of digital platforms. This phenomenon reflects a fundamental tension between shareholder value and user experience that has become endemic across the technology sector.

Unlike traditional businesses that must maintain quality to retain customers, digital platforms benefit from network effects that create natural monopolies. Once a platform achieves dominance, users find it difficult to leave because their social and professional networks are already established there.

This market position allows platforms to gradually degrade their services while maintaining their user base. The process typically involves dark patterns — deliberately deceptive interfaces that prioritise company goals over user preferences.

The enshittification process also reveals how surveillance capitalism operates in practice. Platforms extract value not just from direct payments, but from the detailed behavioural data they collect about their users.

Enshittification represents a manifestation of deeper structural contradictions within contemporary platform capitalism. The phenomenon illuminates how venture capital funding models, regulatory capture, and market concentration interact to systematically degrade digital services over time.

The underlying logic of platform businesses creates perverse incentives. Platforms must simultaneously serve multiple stakeholder groups with conflicting interests: users who want free, high-quality services; advertisers who want access to user attention and data; and investors who demand exponential growth and profitability.

Regulatory capture compounds these problems by ensuring platforms can operate with minimal oversight during their growth phases. By the time regulators attempt to intervene, platforms have achieved such market dominance that meaningful competition becomes nearly impossible.

The enshittification process also reflects the broader tension between use value and exchange value in digital commodities. While platforms initially prioritise use value to attract users, the imperative for capital accumulation ultimately subordinates user needs to profit maximisation.

The Three Stages of Decline

According to Doctorow, enshittification happens in three clear stages. Understanding these stages helps explain why so many platforms follow the same pattern of getting worse over time.

  • Stage 1 – Attract Users: In the beginning, platforms focus completely on user experience. They offer great features for free and make their services as attractive as possible. The goal is to get as many people as possible to join.
  • Stage 2 – Attract Businesses: Once the platform has millions of users, it starts focusing on businesses and advertisers. The platform begins changing its features to help businesses reach customers, even if this makes the experience less enjoyable for regular users.
  • Stage 3 – Extract Maximum Value: Finally, the platform stops caring about users or businesses and focuses only on making as much money as possible. At this point, both users and businesses are unhappy, but the platform has become so powerful that it is difficult to find alternatives.

Doctorow's analysis identifies a consistent pattern across multiple platform types. This predictable sequence suggests that enshittification isn't accidental but rather an inevitable result of how platform businesses are structured and funded.

  • Stage One – User Acquisition: Platforms operate at a loss to build market share. They offer superior services and generous terms to attract critical mass for network effects to take hold.
  • Stage Two – Business Customer Focus: Once user growth plateaus, platforms shift their attention to monetisation strategies. They introduce advertising and develop tools that help businesses reach consumers, degrading user experience in the process.
  • Stage Three – Value Extraction: Platforms exploit their market dominance to maximise short-term profits. Both users and business customers become dissatisfied, but switching costs and lack of viable alternatives keep them locked in.

The enshittification process relies heavily on sophisticated applications of behavioural economics and algorithmic governance. Platforms exploit cognitive biases and psychological vulnerabilities to maintain user engagement even as service quality deteriorates.

  • Variable Ratio Reinforcement: Variable ratio reinforcement schedules, borrowed from gambling psychology, are embedded throughout platform design. Features like infinite scroll, push notifications, and algorithmic content curation create addiction-like engagement patterns.
  • Nudge Architecture: The opacity of algorithmic systems enables platforms to gradually modify user behaviour without explicit consent. Nudge architecture becomes increasingly manipulative as platforms prioritise engagement metrics over user autonomy or well-being.
  • Data Asymmetry: Data asymmetry between platforms and users creates fundamental power imbalances. Platforms possess comprehensive behavioural profiles that enable precise manipulation, while users lack visibility into how their data is used to influence their decisions.

Real Examples

Let me share some specific examples of how platforms I use have changed over the years.

Facebook/Meta: When I first joined Facebook, it was just a way to connect with friends. The news feed showed posts in chronological order, and there were almost no advertisements. Today, the algorithm decides what I see, most posts are from pages rather than friends, and there are ads everywhere.

YouTube: YouTube used to have just one short ad before some videos. Now there are often two ads at the beginning, ads in the middle, and ads at the end. Many content creators complain that the platform's changes have made it harder for them to reach their audience.

Amazon: Amazon started as a simple online bookstore with great customer service. The site is now full of sponsored products, fake reviews, and complex return policies. It has become much harder to find genuine product reviews or the best deals.

Examining specific examples of enshittification helps illustrate how this process manifests across different types of digital services.

Social Media Transformation: Facebook exemplifies classic enshittification through its evolution from a college networking tool to a complex engagement optimisation machine. The introduction of algorithmic feeds prioritised content that generated strong emotional responses, leading to increased polarisation and misinformation.

Video Platform Evolution: YouTube's transformation demonstrates how creator-focused platforms can turn against their content producers. The platform now implements complex, often arbitrary content moderation policies that creators struggle to navigate. The increase in ad frequency and the introduction of premium subscriptions have fundamentally altered the viewing experience.

E-commerce Degradation: Amazon's marketplace shows how enshittification affects commerce platforms. The integration of sponsored products into search results makes it difficult to find the best deals, while the proliferation of review manipulation undermines consumer trust. The platform now functions more as an advertising medium than a neutral marketplace.

The durability of enshittified platforms depends on sophisticated switching costs and network effects that create artificial barriers to competition.

Network Lock-in: Metcalfe's Law suggests that network value increases exponentially with user adoption, but enshittification reveals how this relationship can be exploited. Once platforms achieve critical mass, they can extract value from network effects while simultaneously degrading the experience that initially created those effects.

Data Portability Limitations: Users accumulate digital assets — social connections, content libraries, purchase histories — that become difficult or impossible to transfer to alternative platforms. This creates switching costs that persist even when users become deeply dissatisfied with service quality.

Envelopment Strategy: The phenomenon of envelopment strategy further entrenches dominant platforms. By expanding into adjacent services and integrating them with core offerings, platforms create complex ecosystems that increase switching costs and reduce the viability of specialised competitors.

The Bigger Picture

Enshittification doesn't just make apps more annoying — it actually changes how we live and communicate. When platforms become focused on engagement rather than user satisfaction, it affects our relationships and mental health.

Social media algorithms now promote content that makes people angry or emotional because these posts get more comments and shares. This means we see more negative news and controversial opinions, which can make us feel more stressed and divided from other people.

The constant notifications and infinite scroll features are designed to be addictive. Many people find it difficult to stop using these platforms, even when they are no longer enjoying the experience. This can interfere with work, sleep, and personal relationships.

Understanding enshittification helps us make better decisions about which platforms to use and how to use them. We can look for alternative services, adjust our privacy settings, and demand better from the companies that provide these services.

The widespread nature of enshittification raises important questions about digital infrastructure and its role in modern society. As these platforms become essential for communication, commerce, and information access, their degradation affects not just individual users but entire communities and democratic processes.

Digital divide issues become more pronounced when platforms prioritise profit over accessibility. Features that once made services inclusive are removed or placed behind paywalls, effectively excluding users who cannot pay for premium access.

The concentration of digital power in the hands of a few large platforms creates systemic risks for information flow and economic activity. When these platforms change their policies or algorithms, the effects ripple through society, affecting everything from small business marketing to political discourse.

Potential solutions include stronger antitrust enforcement, data portability requirements, and support for decentralised alternatives. However, the path forward requires balancing innovation incentives with user protection — a challenge that policymakers around the world are still struggling to address effectively.

The enshittification phenomenon poses fundamental challenges to democratic governance and market regulation. Traditional antitrust frameworks, designed for industrial-era monopolies, prove inadequate for addressing the complex dynamics of platform-mediated markets.

Interoperability mandates represent one promising regulatory approach, potentially reducing switching costs and enabling meaningful competition. However, implementation requires sophisticated technical standards and ongoing oversight that many regulatory bodies lack the capacity to provide effectively.

The concept of digital rights emerges as platforms become essential infrastructure for civic participation. When enshittified platforms control access to information, social connection, and economic opportunity, service degradation becomes a broader question of social justice and democratic participation.

Alternative organisational models, including platform cooperatives and public digital infrastructure, offer potential pathways beyond the enshittification cycle. However, scaling these alternatives requires addressing fundamental questions about funding, governance, and technological capacity that remain largely unresolved.
