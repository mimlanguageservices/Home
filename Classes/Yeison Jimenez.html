What is Neurodivergency?

Neurodivergency refers to natural variations in how human brains work. The term recognises that there is no single "correct" way for a brain to function. People whose brains work differently from what society considers "typical" are called neurodivergent. This includes people with autism, ADHD, dyslexia, and other neurological differences. Rather than viewing these differences as disorders to be fixed, neurodiversity celebrates them as part of natural human variation - much like having different eye colours or being left-handed.

Autism as a Spectrum

Autism is described as a "spectrum" because it affects people in vastly different ways. The spectrum is not a line from "mild" to "severe" - instead, think of it as a colour wheel with many different dimensions. One autistic person might excel at verbal communication but struggle with sensory processing. Another might have exceptional memory but find social situations overwhelming.

  • Social communication differences
  • Sensory sensitivities (light, sound, texture)
  • Deep, focused interests
  • Preference for routines and predictability

Understanding ADHD

ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder) affects how people regulate attention, impulses, and energy levels. Despite the name, people with ADHD don't lack attention - they often struggle to direct it where needed. They may hyperfocus intensely on interesting tasks while finding routine tasks extremely difficult. ADHD brains often seek stimulation and novelty, leading to creativity and quick thinking, but also challenges with organisation and time management.

Common Patterns and Strengths

Neurodivergent people often share certain traits that can be both challenging and advantageous. Many experience "masking" - hiding their natural behaviours to fit in, which can be exhausting. However, neurodivergent minds often bring unique strengths: pattern recognition, creative problem-solving, attention to detail, honest communication, and the ability to think outside conventional boundaries. Many successful scientists, artists, and entrepreneurs are neurodivergent.

Different Brains, Different Strengths

Everyone's brain is unique. Some people's brains work differently from most other people. We call these people neurodivergent. This is not a bad thing - it is just different.

People with autism often notice small details that others miss. They might have a favourite topic that they know very well. Some autistic people don't like loud noises or bright lights.

People with ADHD have lots of energy and many ideas. They can be very creative. Sometimes they find it hard to sit still or wait their turn.

Neurodivergent people have many strengths. They see the world in interesting ways. We can all learn from each other!

Understanding Neurodiversity

The word "neurodiversity" comes from two words: "neuro" (brain) and "diversity" (variety). It's the idea that human brains naturally come in many different types, and that's perfectly normal. Just as people have different personalities, people have different brain types too.

Autism is often described as a "spectrum." This means that autism affects people in many different ways. Some autistic people need a lot of support in daily life, while others live independently. What they share is a different way of experiencing the world - often more intensely than others.

ADHD affects how people pay attention and regulate their energy. People with ADHD might find boring tasks extremely difficult, but they can focus for hours on something that interests them. This is called "hyperfocus."

Other types of neurodivergence include dyslexia (difficulty with reading), dyspraxia (difficulty with movement and coordination), and dyscalculia (difficulty with numbers).

Understanding neurodiversity helps us create a more inclusive world where everyone can succeed.

The Neurodiversity Paradigm

The neurodiversity movement has fundamentally changed how we understand neurological differences. Rather than viewing conditions like autism and ADHD as deficits to be corrected, the neurodiversity paradigm recognises them as natural variations in human cognition.

The autism spectrum is perhaps better understood as a "constellation" of traits rather than a linear scale. An autistic person might have exceptional abilities in pattern recognition and systematic thinking while experiencing significant challenges with sensory processing. Another might be highly verbal but struggle with executive function tasks like organisation and planning.

ADHD, similarly, is characterised by differences in dopamine regulation that affect motivation and attention. While neurotypical individuals can often force themselves to complete uninteresting tasks, ADHD brains require more stimulation to engage. This isn't laziness - it's neurology.

Many neurodivergent people engage in "masking" - suppressing their natural behaviours to fit in with neurotypical expectations. While this can help in certain situations, chronic masking often leads to burnout and mental health challenges. Increasingly, advocates argue for accommodation rather than assimilation.

The workplace is gradually adapting to neurodiversity. Forward-thinking companies recognise that neurodivergent employees often bring unique strengths: innovation, attention to detail, and the ability to spot patterns others miss.

Neurodivergence: Challenging the Medical Model

The concept of neurodiversity represents a paradigm shift from the traditional medical model of disability. Where the medical model frames neurological differences as pathologies requiring treatment, the neurodiversity framework positions them as natural variations that have always existed within the human population - variations that may actually confer evolutionary advantages.

The spectrum conceptualisation of autism has evolved considerably since its inception. Contemporary understanding rejects the notion of a linear progression from "high-functioning" to "low-functioning" - terms now widely considered reductive and pejorative. Instead, autism is increasingly understood as a multidimensional profile where an individual may present with vastly different abilities across domains such as social communication, sensory processing, executive function, and verbal expression.

This heterogeneity explains why autistic individuals can appear so different from one another. A tech entrepreneur who struggles with small talk and an individual requiring full-time care may share the same diagnosis, yet their lived experiences diverge dramatically.

ADHD presents similar complexity. The nomenclature itself is contested - "deficit" implies something lacking, when in reality ADHD brains simply allocate attention differently. The ability to hyperfocus on stimulating activities whilst struggling with mundane tasks reflects differences in dopaminergic reward systems, not moral failing or insufficient effort.

The phenomenon of masking - also termed "camouflaging" - has received considerable research attention. Studies indicate that chronic masking correlates strongly with anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation, particularly among autistic women, who historically have been underdiagnosed due to presenting differently from the male-centric diagnostic criteria.

The implications for society are profound. Rather than expecting neurodivergent individuals to assimilate into neurotypical norms, the neurodiversity movement advocates for environmental and societal adaptations that enable all neurotypes to thrive. This represents not merely accommodation but a fundamental reimagining of what we consider "normal" human cognition.

Understanding the Spectrum

The autism spectrum includes people with very different abilities and challenges. No two autistic people are exactly alike.

  • Why do you think autism is called a "spectrum"?
  • What are the dangers of using labels like "mild" or "severe" autism?
  • How can we better understand individual differences?

ADHD Misconceptions

Many people misunderstand ADHD. It's not just about being hyperactive or unable to focus - it's about how the brain regulates attention.

  • What common misconceptions about ADHD have you heard?
  • Why might someone with ADHD focus well on video games but struggle with homework?
  • How could schools better support students with ADHD?

Sensory Experiences

Many neurodivergent people experience sensory information differently. What seems normal to one person might be overwhelming to another.

  • Can you think of sounds, lights, or textures that bother you?
  • How might a busy shopping centre feel to someone with sensory sensitivities?
  • What accommodations could help people with sensory differences?

Masking and Authenticity

Masking is when neurodivergent people hide their natural behaviours to seem "normal." This takes a lot of energy.

  • Have you ever felt you had to hide part of who you are? How did it feel?
  • Why might someone feel pressure to mask their differences?
  • How can we create spaces where people feel safe being themselves?

Strengths and Challenges

Neurodivergent people often have unique strengths alongside their challenges. Many successful people in science, art, and business are neurodivergent.

  • What strengths might come with seeing the world differently?
  • Do you know any famous people who are neurodivergent?
  • How can workplaces benefit from neurodivergent employees?

Communication Differences

Neurodivergent people may communicate differently - more directly, or through different channels like writing instead of speaking.

  • What are some different ways people communicate?
  • Is direct communication always bad? When might it be helpful?
  • How can we adapt our communication for different people?

Support and Accommodation

Accommodations are changes that help neurodivergent people succeed. These might include flexible schedules, quiet spaces, or written instructions.

  • What accommodations might help someone with sensory sensitivities at work?
  • Should students with ADHD get extra time on tests? Why or why not?
  • How can small changes make big differences for neurodivergent people?

Creating Inclusive Spaces

An inclusive society benefits everyone. When we design for diversity, we create better environments for all people.

  • What would a truly inclusive classroom or workplace look like?
  • How can we become better allies to neurodivergent people?
  • What changes would you like to see in society regarding neurodiversity?

Vocabulary

Neurodivergent
Having a brain that works differently from what is considered typical
Neurotypical
Having a brain that functions in the way society considers typical or standard
Spectrum
A range of different conditions or traits, not a single fixed state
Autism
A neurological difference affecting social communication and sensory processing
ADHD
Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder - affects focus, impulse control, and energy
Dyslexia
A learning difference that affects reading, writing, and spelling abilities
Accommodation
Changes or adjustments made to help someone participate fully
Inclusive
Welcoming and accessible to all people, regardless of differences
Masking
Hiding natural behaviours to appear more neurotypical or fit in
Hyperfocus
Intense concentration on one activity, often for long periods
Sensory
Related to the five senses: sight, sound, touch, taste, and smell
Stimming
Repetitive movements or sounds used for self-regulation or comfort
Executive Function
Brain skills for planning, organising, and completing tasks
Burnout
Extreme exhaustion from prolonged stress, often from masking
