Who Was Iris Apfel?

Iris Apfel (1921-2024) was an American fashion icon, interior designer, and businesswoman who became famous for her bold, eclectic style and oversized round glasses. She lived to be 102 years old and became more famous in her later years than ever before. Her fearless approach to fashion and her philosophy that "more is more" made her an inspiration to people of all ages around the world.

Early Life and Career

Born in Queens, New York in 1921, Iris grew up surrounded by fashion. Her mother owned a fashion boutique, and young Iris loved visiting flea markets and second-hand stores to find unique treasures. She studied art history at New York University and later worked in interior design, eventually starting a textile company with her husband Carl.

  • Designed interiors for nine U.S. presidents at the White House
  • Travelled the world sourcing rare textiles and fabrics
  • Built an enormous personal collection of clothing and accessories
  • Always mixed high-end couture with bargain finds

Late Fame and Legacy

In 2005, when Iris was 84, the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York held an exhibition of her clothing collection. The show was a huge success and made her an overnight sensation. She went on to become a documentary subject, model, and social media star. At 97, she signed a modelling contract and continued working until her death in 2024.

Her Philosophy on Style

Iris believed that style was about self-expression, not following rules. She famously said, "When you don't dress like everybody else, you don't have to think like everybody else." She mixed patterns that clashed, wore layers of jewelry, and combined items from different eras and cultures. For Iris, fashion was about having fun and being curious about yourself.

Iris Apfel: A Fashion Icon

Iris Apfel was one of the most striking figures in the fashion world. Born in Queens, New York in 1921, she developed a love for fashion from her mother, who owned a fashion boutique. As a child, Iris spent hours exploring flea markets and second-hand stores, searching for bargains and unusual pieces.

After studying art history at university, Iris began working in fashion and interior design. In 1950, she married Carl Apfel, and together they started Old World Weavers, a textile company that manufactured and restored historic fabrics. Their work was so respected that they were hired to design interiors for the White House during nine different presidencies.

On her extensive travels abroad, Apfel picked up any unusual clothing, jewellery and accessories which caught her eye. She was fearless in her combinations, often wearing patterns that clashed and mixing couture items with pieces bought off-the-rack.

In 2005, the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York based a travelling exhibition on her clothing collection. Visitors loved it and their rave reviews online made Apfel, then in her eighties, into a household name.

Iris Apfel: Breaking Fashion's Rules

Iris Apfel, who passed away in 2024 at the age of 102, was a trailblazer who challenged conventional ideas about fashion, age, and beauty. Her trademark oversized round glasses, which she likened to owl eyes, became as recognisable as any designer logo.

Born to a Russian immigrant mother who ran a fashion boutique, Apfel cultivated her distinctive aesthetic from childhood. She studied art history at New York University before embarking on a career that would span interior design, textile manufacturing, and eventually fashion consultancy. With her husband Carl, she founded Old World Weavers, a company that specialised in reproducing historical textiles for restoration projects, including work on the White House.

What set Apfel apart was her fearless approach to getting dressed. Nothing was off-limits – she was happy to wear clashing prints or costume jewellery even to quite formal social occasions. She might pair a historic opera costume with casual pyjama trousers, finishing the whole thing off with plenty of "bling" – her word for the elaborate jewellery she piled on in layers.

Her late-in-life fame was largely unprecedented. The 2005 exhibition at the Costume Institute of the Metropolitan Museum of Art introduced her to a global audience, and the 2014 documentary "Iris" by Albert Maysles cemented her status as a cultural icon. At 97, she signed a modelling contract with IMG, becoming one of the oldest professional models in history.

Apfel was outspoken about her philosophy on style. "I'm not a pretty person. I don't like pretty, so I don't feel badly," she once said. "Great personal style is an extreme curiosity about yourself." Much of her enormous collection was sold off before she died so that others could enjoy the items as much as she had.

Iris Apfel: The Accidental Icon

In an industry preoccupied with youth and conventional beauty standards, Iris Apfel's ascent to fashion stardom in her ninth decade represented a paradigm shift. Her trajectory from respected but relatively obscure interior designer to global fashion icon offers a fascinating case study in how authenticity and irreverence can triumph over traditional gatekeeping.

Apfel's aesthetic sensibility was shaped by her formative years in Astoria, Queens, where her mother Sadye operated a fashion boutique catering to discerning clientele. This early exposure to the mechanics of retail and the nuances of personal style would prove foundational. Her penchant for scouring flea markets and thrift stores – a practice that would later become synonymous with her brand – began in childhood, driven as much by economic necessity as aesthetic curiosity.

The textile business she established with her husband Carl in 1950 required extensive international travel, and it was during these journeys that Apfel amassed the extraordinary collection that would eventually make her famous. She approached acquisition with an egalitarian eye, seeing no contradiction in pairing haute couture with street market finds, or juxtaposing ethnic textiles from disparate cultures. This democratisation of fashion – the idea that style transcends price tags – would become central to her philosophy.

The 2005 exhibition at the Costume Institute, initially conceived as a modest showcase, exceeded all expectations and catalysed Apfel's transformation from connoisseur to celebrity. The timing proved serendipitous: social media was emerging as a democratising force in fashion, and Apfel's visual impact translated perfectly to platforms like Instagram, where her bold aesthetic and aphoristic wisdom about style garnered millions of followers.

Perhaps most significantly, Apfel challenged the fashion industry's entrenched ageism. Her visibility as a working model and brand ambassador in her nineties and beyond forced a reconsideration of who could be considered fashionable. "When you don't dress like everybody else, you don't have to think like everybody else," she famously declared – a manifesto for individuality that resonated across generations.

Age and Fashion

Iris became a model at 97 years old, challenging ideas about who can be fashionable.

  • Do you think there are clothes that older people "shouldn't" wear? Why or why not?
  • Why do you think Iris became MORE famous as she got older?
  • How does your culture view older people and fashion?

Breaking Fashion Rules

Iris mixed patterns that clashed and combined expensive designer items with cheap finds from markets.

  • What "fashion rules" have you heard of? (e.g., "don't mix patterns")
  • Do you follow these rules or break them? Give an example.
  • Iris said, "More is more." Do you agree or prefer "less is more"?

Fashion as Self-Expression

  • What do you think she meant by this quote?

Collecting and Memories

Iris collected clothes and accessories from around the world for over 70 years.

Redefining Beauty

Finding Treasures
