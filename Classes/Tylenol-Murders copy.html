United Airlines

What Happened?

On April 9, 2017, United Airlines Flight 3411 was ready to leave Chicago for Louisville, Kentucky. The flight was full. The airline needed four seats for its own employees. They asked passengers to leave the plane voluntarily, but nobody wanted to go.

United then selected four passengers randomly. One of them, Dr. David Dao, refused to leave. He said he had patients to see the next morning. Security officers were called. They physically dragged him off the plane. He was injured — his face was bloody, and he lost two teeth.

Other passengers filmed the whole thing on their phones. The videos were posted online and went viral within hours. Millions of people around the world saw what happened, and they were shocked. United Airlines was suddenly in the middle of a very serious crisis.

On April 9, 2017, United Express Flight 3411 was overbooked at Chicago O'Hare International Airport. The airline needed to transport four of its own crew members to Louisville for another flight. After offering travel vouchers with no volunteers coming forward, United randomly selected four passengers to be removed.

One passenger, Dr. David Dao, a 69-year-old physician, refused to give up his seat, stating he had patients to attend to the following morning. Chicago Aviation Security officers were called and forcibly removed him from the aircraft. He was dragged down the aisle, sustaining a concussion, a broken nose, and two lost teeth.

Fellow passengers filmed the removal on their smartphones. The footage spread rapidly across social media, creating an immediate and catastrophic backlash. Within 24 hours, United Airlines found itself at the centre of a global PR disaster.

The events of April 9, 2017 aboard United Express Flight 3411 exposed a series of systemic failures across multiple levels of the airline industry. The core incident — the forced removal of a ticketed, seated passenger — was technically permitted under United's Contract of Carriage and US Department of Transportation regulations governing involuntary denied boarding. However, the application of those rules, combined with the use of law enforcement to enforce a commercial staffing requirement, transformed a routine overbooking situation into a reputational catastrophe.

Dr. David Dao's forcible removal — captured on video by multiple passengers and immediately uploaded to social media — illustrated the profound shift in corporate accountability that smartphone cameras and social platforms have produced. What was previously a private incident between an airline and a single passenger became, within hours, a globally witnessed spectacle subject to immediate and intense public judgment.

The speed and scale of the resulting backlash outpaced every layer of United's institutional response capacity — legal, communications, and executive — creating a crisis whose initial trajectory was shaped entirely by citizen journalism rather than corporate narrative management.

United's Initial Response

After the video went viral, United Airlines CEO Oscar Munoz sent a letter to employees. In the letter, he called Dr. Dao "disruptive and belligerent." He did not apologize to Dr. Dao. He defended what the security officers had done. Many people were very angry with this response.

United's first public statement said they were sorry that passengers had to be "re-accommodated." Many people criticized this word — "re-accommodated" is a very formal, indirect way to say "forced off the plane." It did not sound sincere.

The company's reputation got worse every hour. Customers were angry. Journalists criticized the airline. United's stock price fell. Many people said they would never fly United again. The company had made the crisis much worse with its bad communication.

United's crisis response unfolded in damaging stages. CEO Oscar Munoz's initial statement expressed regret only for "having to re-accommodate" passengers — a euphemism that was instantly and widely mocked. He did not apologize to Dr. Dao, and his internal email to employees praised staff for following "established procedures" — a message that quickly leaked to the press.

The sequence of responses made things worse at every step. Munoz's first public statement defended the removal. His second statement, the following day, offered a limited apology. Only his third statement — issued after the story had dominated global news for 48 hours — was a genuine, unconditional apology.

The timing was critical. By the time a sincere apology arrived, United had already lost control of the narrative. The airline's brand damage compounded with each hour of delay, and each defensive statement was used as evidence of the company's indifference to its customers.

United's initial crisis communications represent a textbook example of what crisis theorists classify as a "defensive" response strategy — one that prioritises institutional protection over victim welfare. Munoz's first statement deployed the passive voice ("Re-accommodation"), minimising language ("re-accommodated"), and procedural justification ("following established procedures") — all of which crisis communication research consistently identifies as markers that amplify rather than contain public anger.

The internal employee email — in which Munoz commended staff and described Dao as "disruptive and belligerent" — represented a catastrophic failure of communication discipline. Its rapid public disclosure transformed what might have been a containable internal communication into documentary proof of the company's true institutional posture, destroying the credibility of any subsequent apologetic statement.

Compounding the structural failures was a deeply incongruous piece of context: Munoz had been named PRWeek's "Communicator of the Year" only weeks before the incident. The irony was seized upon gleefully by media outlets worldwide and became a recurring element of coverage, further intensifying the reputational damage.

The Viral Video Effect

The videos filmed by passengers on Flight 3411 changed everything. Before the internet and smartphones, this kind of incident might never have become public. But in 2017, anyone with a phone can record a video and share it with millions of people in seconds.

The footage was shocking to watch. People shared it on Twitter, Facebook, and other social media platforms. Within 24 hours, the video had been seen millions of times. By the next day, it was on the news in every country.

United Airlines' stock fell by $1.4 billion in value in just one day. The hashtag "#BoycottUnited" was used hundreds of thousands of times on Twitter. This shows how quickly a viral video can destroy a company's reputation.

The passenger removal video illustrated the power of social media amplification in the modern era. What previously might have been resolved quietly between an airline and a passenger became — within hours — a globally shared emotional experience that tens of millions of people reacted to in real time.

The footage spread faster than United's communications team could respond. By the time the company issued its first statement, the public narrative was already fully formed: United had physically assaulted a paying customer. Every subsequent corporate statement was evaluated against that pre-existing frame.

The financial consequences were immediate. United Airlines' stock dropped by approximately $1.4 billion in market capitalisation in a single trading day. The hashtag "#BoycottUnited" trended globally. Chinese social media was particularly active — Dr. Dao was of Vietnamese-Chinese heritage — and the story generated enormous backlash in an important market for the airline.

The United Airlines incident is a defining case study in the mechanics of viral crisis escalation. The footage simultaneously fulfilled several conditions that crisis communication researchers identify as predictors of maximum viral spread: it was emotionally visceral, easily comprehensible without context, visually unambiguous in its moral implications, and shareable across multiple platforms in its original form.

Critically, the viral spread created what scholars term a "narrative lock" — a situation in which the dominant public interpretation of events becomes so firmly established so quickly that subsequent corporate communications are structurally unable to alter it. United's defensive first statements did not merely fail to change the narrative; they actively confirmed it, providing new content that the media cycle could absorb and amplify.

The incident also demonstrated the cross-border dimensions of contemporary viral crises. Because Dr. Dao was of Asian heritage, the story generated disproportionate engagement on Chinese social media platforms — including Weibo and WeChat — where it framed the incident in terms of racial treatment of Asian passengers. This dimension added a layer of reputational damage in a market of strategic importance to United.

Describing Sequences of Events

When we tell a story or describe what happened, we use sequence words to make the order clear. Here are the most useful ones:

  • First / To begin with: "First, the airline asked passengers to leave voluntarily."
  • Then / Next / After that: "Then, they selected passengers randomly."
  • When / After: "After Dr. Dao refused, security was called."
  • As a result / So: "As a result, millions of people saw the video."
  • Finally / Eventually / In the end: "Finally, the CEO gave a full apology."

Practice: Use these words to describe the sequence of events on Flight 3411. Try to tell the whole story in five sentences, using one sequence word in each sentence.

In professional and academic English, sequencing events precisely is essential for clear analysis and reporting. The United Airlines crisis offers an ideal sequence to practice with:

  • Prior to / Before: "Prior to the removal, the airline had asked for volunteers twice."
  • Following / After: "Following the forced removal, passengers immediately began filming."
  • Subsequently / Shortly afterwards: "The footage was subsequently shared on social media within minutes."
  • As a consequence / As a result: "As a consequence, United's stock fell $1.4 billion in a single day."
  • Eventually / Ultimately / In the aftermath: "Ultimately, Munoz issued a full and unconditional apology."

Note: Stronger connectors like consequently and as a result imply a clear cause-and-effect relationship — use them only when one event directly caused the next. Weaker connectors like then simply indicate time order.

Sophisticated sequencing in professional discourse goes beyond simple time-order connectors to encode cause, consequence, and evaluation simultaneously. Consider the difference in these descriptions of the United crisis:

  • Temporal only: "The video was posted. Then the stock fell." — indicates order, no causation.
  • Causal: "The viral dissemination of the footage precipitated an immediate collapse in United's market capitalisation." — implies direct causation.
  • Concessive: "Despite United's initial defensive response, public pressure ultimately compelled a full apology." — acknowledges contrast before the expected outcome.
  • Conditional retrospective: "Had United's initial statement been apologetic, the subsequent escalation might have been averted." — counterfactual sequence.

In crisis post-mortems and business case analyses, the choice of sequencing language signals whether the writer is narrating, analysing, or evaluating. Choosing the appropriate register and connector type is a key marker of advanced professional writing — and of genuine accountability in corporate communication.

Discussing Brand Reputation

A reputation is what people think about a company. A good reputation is built slowly. It can be destroyed very quickly. Here are some phrases for talking about brand reputation:

  • "This damaged the company's reputation because..."
  • "Customers lost trust in the brand when..."
  • "The company's image suffered after..."
  • "They were able to rebuild trust by..."
  • "I think this company is/is not trustworthy because..."

Discussion: Use these phrases to talk about United Airlines. Do you think they were able to rebuild their reputation after 2017? What would you do differently if you were the CEO?

In business English, discussing brand damage and recovery requires a specific set of evaluative phrases. Here are the most useful categories:

  • Describing harm: "The incident significantly undermined consumer confidence." / "United's brand image took a severe hit." / "The airline's reputation suffered irreparably in the short term."
  • Describing cause: "The damage was compounded by the CEO's failure to apologize immediately." / "The defensive response accelerated the crisis rather than containing it."
  • Describing recovery: "United attempted to rebuild trust through policy reform." / "The airline introduced new procedures to demonstrate its commitment to passengers."
  • Evaluating outcome: "Whether United's reputation fully recovered is still debated." / "The long-term brand damage is difficult to quantify."

Key language note: Use the passive voice to describe reputation damage when the focus is on the company, not the cause: "United's reputation was damaged." Use active voice when assigning responsibility: "United's defensive statements worsened the crisis."

Analytical discourse about brand equity and reputational risk operates at the intersection of marketing, finance, and crisis communication theory. Key analytical constructs include:

  • Reputational capital: The accumulated trust and goodwill a brand holds — United's was substantial but failed to act as a buffer because the incident was so visceral.
  • Trust repair: Research distinguishes between cognitive trust (belief in competence) and affective trust (emotional confidence). United damaged both simultaneously — competence trust through the operational failure; affective trust through its communications.
  • Narrative ownership: In the social media era, companies rarely control the dominant narrative of a crisis. Language choices that acknowledge this reality — "we understand why our response fell short of what you deserved" — signal self-awareness and tend to perform better with audiences than language that attempts to redirect or reframe.

Discussion: Compare United's crisis to Johnson & Johnson's 1982 Tylenol response. Both companies faced situations largely outside their control. What structural differences explain the divergence in their reputational damage?

The Apology & Policy Changes

Two days after the incident, CEO Oscar Munoz finally gave a real apology. He said: "I deeply apologize to Dr. Dao and to all the customers aboard. No one should ever be mistreated this way." This was the first time he said sorry directly to Dr. Dao by name.

United Airlines also changed its rules. The airline said it would no longer use police to remove passengers from overbooked flights. It increased the maximum compensation offered to passengers who voluntarily give up their seats — up to $10,000. Dr. Dao settled his legal case with United for an undisclosed amount.

The US Congress also held hearings about passenger rights. Airlines were required to clearly explain passenger rights at check-in and at the gate. The incident changed the rules for all airlines in the United States.

Munoz's final statement, issued April 11, 2017, marked a clear shift in tone: "I deeply apologize to Dr. Dao and to all the customers aboard. No one should ever be mistreated this way. I want you to know that we take full accountability and will work to make it right." This was the first statement to use Dr. Dao's name and to take unambiguous personal responsibility.

United subsequently implemented a series of policy reforms: law enforcement would no longer be used to remove boarded, seated passengers on overbooked flights; maximum voluntary bump compensation was raised to $10,000; and crew re-accommodation planning would be completed before passengers boarded. Dr. Dao reached a confidential financial settlement with the airline.

At the Congressional level, the incident prompted hearings on airline industry practices and resulted in the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018, which strengthened passenger rights disclosures and tightened the rules around involuntary denied boarding across the US airline industry.

The evolution of Munoz's public statements over 48 hours offers a compelling case study in the rhetoric of corporate contrition under pressure. The progression from euphemistic deflection ("re-accommodate") to procedural justification ("established procedures") to unconditional apology mirrors a pattern crisis communication scholars term "escalating accommodation" — a response trajectory driven by external pressure rather than internal ethical commitment, and therefore perceived by audiences as reactive rather than genuine.

The policy reforms United announced were substantive and genuinely consequential: banning law enforcement use for commercial overbooking, raising voluntary bump compensation to $10,000, and restructuring crew scheduling to avoid last-minute displacement of boarded passengers. However, because these reforms arrived after the reputational damage was severe, they functioned primarily as damage-limitation measures rather than trust-rebuilding instruments.

The broader regulatory response — embedded in the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018 — established clearer passenger rights requirements across the industry. Unlike J&J's proactive engagement with regulators in 1982, United's engagement with Congressional oversight was reactive — another structural difference that crisis communication theory would predict as correlating with diminished long-term brand equity recovery.

Lessons for Business

The United Airlines incident teaches companies some very important lessons:

  • Apologize quickly. Every hour without a real apology made things worse. A sincere "sorry" on the first day could have saved the company from much of the damage.
  • Put customers first. United defended its rules instead of its customer. This was the biggest mistake.
  • Think about your language. Calling the removal "re-accommodation" made people angrier. Use honest, clear words.
  • Be careful what you write in emails. Munoz's letter to employees was leaked. Everything a CEO writes can become public.
  • Your customers have cameras. In 2017, every passenger is also a journalist. Any incident can become a viral video instantly.

The United Airlines crisis generated several durable lessons for corporate communications and customer service strategy:

  • Speed of apology matters enormously. Research shows that the first 24 hours of a viral crisis are decisive. United's delayed, defensive first response established a damaging narrative that was extremely difficult to reverse.
  • Internal communications can become public. Munoz's employee email was never intended for public consumption. Companies must assume that any written communication can be leaked and read by the public.
  • Language signals values. "Re-accommodate" was heard as evasion. In a crisis, audiences are extremely sensitive to language that appears to minimize harm or avoid accountability.
  • Policy must match communication. An apology without policy change is unconvincing. United's credibility improved only when concrete reforms accompanied its words.
  • The passenger is now the journalist. Crisis communication strategies must account for real-time video documentation by customers.

The United Airlines incident has become the canonical counter-example to J&J's 1982 Tylenol response — the two cases now taught together as defining the boundaries of effective and ineffective crisis communication. The structural contrast is analytically instructive:

  • Pre-existing values framework: J&J had its Credo; United had no equivalent customer-first principle that constrained executive decision-making in the moment of crisis. The absence of a documented values hierarchy left United's communications team with no institutional authority to override legal caution with ethical clarity.
  • Speed as trust signal: J&J moved faster than required; United moved slower than public expectation demanded. In the social media era, the gap between incident and response is measured in minutes, not days.
  • The impossibility of narrative management in the smartphone era: United's crisis underscores that companies can no longer control the initial framing of a crisis. The strategic implication is that companies must invest in pre-crisis reputation building — brand equity that can buffer the inevitable gap between incident and institutional response.

Key Takeaways

  • The incident: On April 9, 2017, Dr. David Dao was physically dragged off a United Airlines flight to make room for crew members. Passengers filmed it and the video went viral.
  • The bad response: CEO Oscar Munoz did not apologize immediately. He called Dr. Dao "disruptive." This made the crisis much worse.
  • The real apology: Two days later, Munoz gave a sincere apology and named Dr. Dao directly. United changed its policies.
  • The lesson: Apologize quickly, use honest language, and put customers first. Every customer is now a potential journalist with a camera.
  • Language focus: Use sequence words (first, then, after that, as a result, finally) to tell a story clearly. Use phrases like "the company's reputation suffered because..." to discuss brand image.
  • The crisis: A routine overbooking situation escalated into a global PR disaster when security officers forcibly removed a boarded passenger and other passengers filmed it.
  • The communication failure: United's three-stage response — defensive, partially apologetic, then fully apologetic — was too slow. Each delay amplified the backlash.
  • Sequencing language: Use connectors like subsequently, as a consequence, prior to, in the aftermath to describe events precisely. Strong connectors imply causation; weak connectors indicate time order only.
  • Brand reputation language: "The incident significantly undermined consumer confidence" / "United's brand image took a severe hit" / "The airline attempted to rebuild trust through policy reform."
  • Policy changes: United banned police use for overbooking, raised bump compensation to $10,000, and settled with Dr. Dao. Congress strengthened passenger rights across the airline industry.
  • Defensive crisis response: United's strategy — procedural justification followed by escalating apology under external pressure — produced the worst possible outcome: it appeared to confirm public suspicions of institutional indifference while sacrificing the credibility benefits of proactive accommodation.
  • The smartphone era: The incident demonstrated that companies can no longer manage the initial narrative of a crisis. Pre-crisis brand equity is now the primary buffer against catastrophic reputational damage.
  • Sequencing in professional discourse: The choice of discourse connector encodes causation, evaluation, and stance. "Subsequently" narrates; "consequently" implies causation; "despite" signals concession. These distinctions are analytically and rhetorically significant.
  • Contrast with J&J 1982: The divergence between United's and J&J's crisis responses illustrates the enabling role of pre-existing institutional values. Without a documented customer-first framework, United's crisis communications were structurally unable to signal authentic commitment to passenger welfare.
  • Legacy: The FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018 and United's internal policy reforms produced lasting improvements in passenger rights protections — though, unlike J&J's proactive regulatory engagement, these were reactive rather than crisis-defining.

In April 2017, United Airlines CEO Oscar Munoz issued a statement defending the airline after a passenger was forcibly removed from an overbooked flight. The video had already been seen by millions of people worldwide. Imagine you are United Airlines' Head of Communications, advising the CEO on the morning after the incident. Write an official public statement of approximately 200–250 words addressed to United's customers. Your statement should: (1) apologize sincerely and directly to Dr. Dao by name; (2) use sequence language to describe clearly what the airline is doing in response; and (3) include language about the company's commitment to rebuilding its reputation and customer trust.
