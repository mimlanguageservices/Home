How Gen Z Is Choosing Cannabis Over Cocktails

The Sober Generation

Gen Z — people born after 1996 — drinks much less alcohol than older generations. Studies show that about 30% of young adults in the UK do not drink at all.

This is a big change. Young people say they prefer to stay sober because they care more about their health and wellness. For many, not drinking is now seen as normal and even cool.

Research consistently shows that Gen Z is consuming significantly less alcohol than previous generations. A 2023 study found that nearly 30% of Gen Z adults in the UK identify as non-drinkers — more than double the rate of Baby Boomers at the same age.

This trend is being driven by rising health consciousness, the influence of social media wellness culture, and the growing stigma around excessive drinking. The "sober curious" movement, which encourages people to question their relationship with alcohol, is growing rapidly across Europe and North America.

A confluence of sociocultural, legislative, and pharmacological factors has precipitated a marked decline in alcohol consumption among Gen Z cohorts globally. Epidemiological data indicates that the proportion of abstinent young adults has risen substantially, with longitudinal studies suggesting a fundamental realignment of social attitudes toward intoxication.

This generational disenchantment with alcohol — rooted in heightened awareness of its links to addiction, mental health deterioration, and productivity loss — represents one of the most consequential behavioural shifts in the beverage industry's history.

Why Cannabis?

Many young people are choosing cannabis instead of alcohol. The biggest reason is simple: cannabis does not give you a hangover.

  • No hangover: You feel fine the next morning
  • Becoming legal: Cannabis is now legal in Canada, Germany, and many US states
  • Health reasons: Many young people say it feels "lighter" on the body
  • Less pressure: Young people feel more free to choose cannabis without social judgement

Several factors explain why Gen Z is increasingly turning to cannabis as a substitute for alcohol. Unlike alcohol, cannabis produces no hangover, and many users report feeling more in control of the experience.

The legalization of recreational cannabis in countries like Canada, Germany, and several US states has dramatically reduced the stigma associated with its use. Combined with Gen Z's strong focus on wellness and mental health, cannabis is increasingly perceived as a less damaging recreational choice.

The substitution of cannabis for alcohol among Gen Z reflects a complex interplay of pharmacological preferences, legislative liberalisation, and shifting cultural values. Cannabis' absence of the acute physiological consequences associated with alcohol — notably the hangover — coupled with its growing legal accessibility, positions it as an increasingly normalized recreational alternative.

Furthermore, the proliferation of cannabis-specific wellness narratives on digital platforms has reframed its use from stigmatised deviance to aspirational self-care — a repositioning that alcohol, with its well-documented links to physical harm, struggles to replicate.

Falling Alcohol Sales

Alcohol companies are losing money because fewer young people are buying their products. Beer, wine, and spirits sales have all gone down in recent years.

  • Beer: Sales have fallen sharply in the UK and USA
  • Wine: Young people are buying much less wine
  • Big brands: Companies like Heineken and Budweiser are reporting lower profits

In response, many companies are creating drinks with less alcohol — or no alcohol at all — to try to attract young customers back.

The decline in youth alcohol consumption is having a measurable impact on global revenue. Beer volumes fell by over 5% in key Western markets between 2021 and 2024, with companies like AB InBev and Heineken reporting consecutive quarters of declining sales.

Wine consumption among under-35s has dropped by nearly 25% in the UK. In response, alcohol corporations are investing heavily in cannabis companies and low-alcohol substitutes to offset these losses and maintain relevance with the Gen Z demographic.

The beverage alcohol industry faces unprecedented revenue erosion as Gen Z's consumption patterns diverge sharply from those of preceding cohorts. Incumbent multinationals such as Diageo, AB InBev, and Constellation Brands have reported sequential declines in core product categories, prompting strategic pivots toward adjacencies.

The precipitous contraction of beer volumes in particular has destabilised long-established distribution networks and prompted a fundamental reassessment of long-term growth assumptions within boardrooms across the sector.

Nightlife in Crisis

Nightclubs are closing because fewer young people go out to drink. In the UK, more than half of all nightclubs have closed since 2005. Famous clubs in London, Manchester, and Leeds are shutting their doors forever.

  • Young people stay home more often
  • When they do go out, they spend less on drinks
  • COVID-19 made the problem much worse
  • Many venues cannot pay their bills without alcohol sales

The hospitality sector has been profoundly affected by Gen Z's changing habits. The UK saw over 50% of its nightclubs close between 2005 and 2024 — a trend dramatically accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic. Bar and club owners report that when young people do go out, they often choose alcohol-free drinks or spend significantly less per visit.

Cities like London, Berlin, and Sydney are witnessing a fundamental transformation of their nightlife economies. The traditional model — high-volume alcohol sales sustaining a venue's profitability — is increasingly unworkable when your target demographic is choosing to stay sober.

The precipitous contraction of the nightclub industry represents perhaps the most visible manifestation of Gen Z's disenchantment with alcohol-centric socialising. UK hospitality data reveals a 56% reduction in nightclub venues since 2005, with closures disproportionately affecting mid-market establishments that historically relied on high alcohol margins.

The sector's structural challenges are exacerbated by the proliferation of at-home entertainment options and the normalisation of cannabis use as a domestic social activity, fundamentally undermining the nightclub's traditional value proposition.

The Industry Adapts

Alcohol companies are changing to survive. They are making new types of drinks — and some are even investing in cannabis. Here are some of the biggest changes:

  • No-alcohol beer and wine: Growing very fast in popularity
  • Sober bars: New bars that serve creative drinks with no alcohol
  • Cannabis drinks: Some companies are selling drinks made with cannabis
  • Heineken: Has invested in cannabis company Tilray

The alcohol industry is adapting to survive the shift in consumer behaviour. Heineken has invested in cannabis company Tilray, and AB InBev has launched cannabis-infused beverages in Canada. The market for no- and low-alcohol (NOLO) drinks grew by over 30% between 2020 and 2024, becoming one of the fastest-growing demographic segments in the drinks industry.

"Sober bars" — venues serving premium alcohol-free cocktails and mocktails — are opening in major cities, offering a social atmosphere without the pressure to drink. These changes signal that the revenue model of the entire hospitality sector is being forced to evolve.

Incumbent beverage corporations are executing multidimensional strategic responses to the structural decline in traditional alcohol consumption. Horizontal diversification into the cannabis sector — exemplified by Constellation Brands' multi-billion dollar investment in Canopy Growth — reflects an acknowledgment that the underlying consumer need for social relaxation may be decoupled from alcohol specifically.

Simultaneously, the rapid proliferation of NOLO products represents an attempt to retain Gen Z consumers within familiar commercial channels. The emergence of "sober bars" and cannabis lounges signals a broader commodification of sobriety as a lifestyle identity.

Sobriety as Identity

A big cultural change is happening. Being sober is now seen as a positive choice. Social media influencers talk about "mindful drinking" — thinking carefully about how much alcohol you consume.

  • The hashtag #SoberCurious has billions of views on TikTok
  • Celebrities are openly talking about choosing not to drink
  • Young people feel less social pressure to drink at parties
  • Not drinking is no longer seen as strange or antisocial

A significant cultural shift is underway, with sobriety increasingly embraced as a positive lifestyle choice rather than a limitation. The "sober curious" movement — popularised by books like Ruby Warrington's Sober Curious — encourages people to question their relationship with alcohol without committing to full abstinence.

For many Gen Z individuals, wellness has replaced the pub as the central social institution. The stigma once attached to not drinking has largely evaporated, replaced by a growing social approval of mindful, health-conscious choices that now shape an entire generation's leisure habits.

The normalization of sobriety among Gen Z represents a paradigm shift in how intoxication is culturally valorised. Where previous generations constructed social identity partly through alcohol consumption — with abstinence frequently stigmatised as deviant — contemporary youth culture increasingly frames sobriety as aspirational.

This inversion has been amplified through digital media ecosystems, where wellness content creators have commodified abstinence as a form of self-optimisation. The resulting disenchantment with alcohol's social role catalyses a broader reassessment of drinking culture's place in the social fabric — with significant commercial consequences.

The Future of Nightlife

The future of going out looks very different. New social spaces are being created for people who do not want to drink alcohol. These new venues focus on fun and connection without needing alcohol.

  • Cannabis lounges: Legal social spaces to use cannabis in the USA and Canada
  • Immersive experiences: Venues focused on art, gaming, or theatre
  • Wellness clubs: Places to relax, exercise, and socialise
  • Live music venues: Experience-led nights with less focus on alcohol

The nightlife industry is reinventing itself for a new generation. Cannabis lounges — venues where customers can legally consume cannabis in a social setting — are growing in US states and Canadian cities where legalization has occurred. These spaces represent a direct substitute for the traditional bar or nightclub.

Experience-led venues — immersive theatre, escape rooms, live entertainment — are attracting Gen Z audiences who prioritise memorable experiences over alcohol consumption. Some industry analysts predict that within a decade, a significant proportion of venues currently selling alcohol will have pivoted to cannabis, mocktails, or experience-based entertainment.

The restructuring of nocturnal social space represents one of the most consequential commercial transformations of the decade. The emergent cannabis hospitality sector — encompassing consumption lounges, cannabis-paired dining, and hybrid retail-social experiences — is positioned to capture substantial market share from traditional alcohol-dependent venues, particularly as legalization frameworks expand globally.

However, the pace of this transition remains subject to significant regulatory uncertainty. The proliferation of cannabis social spaces is contingent on legislative developments that vary dramatically by jurisdiction, meaning the incumbent alcohol sector retains considerable structural advantages in markets where cannabis remains criminalised. The disenchantment with alcohol, however, shows no signs of abating regardless of cannabis' legal status.

Key Takeaways

Remember these important points:

  • Gen Z drinks less: About 30% of young adults in the UK don't drink at all
  • Cannabis is popular: No hangover and becoming legal in more places
  • Nightclubs are closing: More than half have shut in the UK since 2005
  • Companies are changing: No-alcohol drinks and cannabis investment are growing fast
  • Being sober is cool: Sobriety is now a positive identity for many young people

Essential takeaways from this lesson:

  • Generational shift: Gen Z's reduced consumption is driven by health awareness, cannabis legalization, and cultural change
  • Revenue pressure: The alcohol industry faces significant decline and is diversifying into NOLO drinks and cannabis
  • Nightlife disruption: Club closures reflect a fundamental change in how Gen Z socialises
  • Cultural normalisation: The sober curious movement has removed the stigma of not drinking
  • New social spaces: Cannabis lounges and experience venues are emerging to replace nightclubs

Salient conclusions:

  • Structural disruption: The convergence of shifting preferences, legislative liberalisation, and normalization of sobriety is reshaping the entire leisure economy
  • Commercial disenchantment: Incumbent alcohol corporations face existential pressure to diversify or become irrelevant to the largest consumer cohort
  • Precipitous venue attrition: The hospitality sector's structural collapse signals broader urban socioeconomic consequences
  • Legislative dependency: The proliferation of cannabis social spaces remains contingent on the pace of global legalization
  • Identity politics of sobriety: The commodification of abstinence as aspirational lifestyle signals a deep cultural inversion
