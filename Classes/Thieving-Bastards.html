Thieving Bastards

Crime, Vocabulary & Rob vs Steal

Types of Thieves

There are many different types of thieves. Each one has a different way of stealing. Have you ever been robbed or had something stolen from you?

  • A pickpocket works in crowded places like markets or public transport
  • A mugger attacks people on the street and takes their phone or wallet
  • A burglar breaks into houses or shops, usually at night
  • A shoplifter steals things from shops without paying

Discussion: Which type of thief do you think is most common in your city? Which is the most dangerous?

The criminal world has a surprisingly rich vocabulary for different types of thieves. Each specialisation reflects distinct skills, risk tolerance, and target preference.

  • A con artist relies on psychological manipulation rather than force — the most cerebral of criminals
  • A cat burglar combines athleticism and stealth to enter through high windows or skylights
  • A safecracker brings technical expertise — listening to tumblers, exploiting mechanical weaknesses
  • A fence is the criminal economy's merchant — converting stolen property into untraceable cash

Discussion: What do you think motivates people to become professional criminals? Is it purely money, or are there other factors?

Criminal typologies reveal as much about society as they do about individual psychology. The specialisation of theft reflects the economic and social conditions of a given era.

  • Recidivists account for a disproportionate share of criminal activity — a small number of persistent offenders drive the majority of acquisitive crime
  • The distinction between opportunistic and premeditated crime is central to criminal law, affecting both the charge and the sentence
  • White-collar criminals — fraudsters, embezzlers, money launderers — often cause greater aggregate financial harm than street thieves, yet are prosecuted far less frequently

Discussion: Is white-collar crime treated too leniently compared to street crime? What systemic factors might explain any disparity?

Grammar: Rob vs Steal

Many English learners confuse rob and steal. They mean similar things, but we use them very differently.

ROB = person or place (the victim)
"They robbed the bank."  /  "He robbed me."
STEAL = an object (the thing taken)
"She stole my wallet."  /  "They stole the painting."

A quick test: if the next word is a person or a place, use rob. If it is a thing, use steal.

Practice:

  • "Someone ___ my car." (stole)
  • "They ___ the jewellery shop." (robbed)
  • "He ___ £500 from his employer." (stole)

The rob/steal distinction is one of the most commonly tested in English exams — and one of the most frequently confused by learners at all levels.

ROB + [person/place] → focus on the victim
"The gang robbed three banks in a single week." / "She was robbed at knifepoint."
STEAL + [object] → focus on what was taken
"They stole €2 million in diamonds." / "Someone stole her identity."

You can combine both: "They robbed the museum and stole three Picassos."

Related legal terms: robbery, burglary, theft.

The rob/steal distinction maps onto a fundamental legal distinction: robbery requires a victim who is present and subject to force or threat; theft (or larceny) does not require the victim's presence.

ROBBERY: force/threat + victim present → "rob" a person or place
"The cashier was robbed at gunpoint." (victim present, immediate threat)
THEFT / LARCENY: no force required → "steal" an object
"The Mona Lisa was stolen from the Louvre in 1911." (no confrontation)

Grand larceny, embezzlement, and extortion each occupy distinct positions in criminal law, reflecting the mode, scale, and relationship between offender and victim.

Tools & Methods

Different thieves use different tools. Here are some of the most common words for the things thieves use:

  • Lockpicks — small metal tools that open locks without a key, silently
  • A crowbar — a heavy metal bar used to force open doors or windows
  • A getaway car — the car waiting outside to help thieves escape fast
  • An accomplice — someone who helps the thief, for example by keeping watch

Discussion: What do you think is the most important thing for a thief — speed, careful planning, or the right tools?

Professional criminals treat their craft with the same attention to planning as any specialist. A well-executed burglary typically involves multiple stages:

  • Reconnaissance: observing the target for days or weeks — learning routines, identifying security gaps, planning entry and exit
  • Tools: lockpicks, glass cutters, signal jammers (to disable wireless alarms), and electronic key cloners
  • The team: a driver, a lookout, and the specialists who enter the building — each with a defined role
  • The exit: a clean getaway, pre-planned escape routes, and a fence already arranged

Discussion: How has technology changed crime — both making it easier to commit and easier to detect and prosecute?

The arms race between criminal methodology and law enforcement capability is continuous and adaptive. Each advance in security technology is met with creative countermeasures.

  • Social engineering has become the most cost-effective attack vector — it is cheaper to deceive a human than to defeat a modern electronic security system
  • Cybercrime blurs traditional legal boundaries: ransomware is extortion; account takeover is theft; identity fraud combines elements of both
  • Cryptocurrency has transformed the laundering of criminal proceeds, posing novel challenges for financial intelligence agencies worldwide

Discussion: Should cybercrime carry the same penalties as physical theft of equivalent value? What are the arguments for and against?

Famous Heists in History

Some robberies are so famous that everyone knows about them. Here are two of the most well-known:

  • The Great Train Robbery (1963): A gang of 17 people stopped a Royal Mail train in England and stole £2.6 million in cash. The loot was never fully recovered. Most of their accomplices were eventually caught.
  • The Mona Lisa (1911): A man stole the most famous painting in the world from the Louvre museum in Paris. He hid it under his coat and walked out! It was missing for two years before he was caught.

Discussion: Why do you think some famous criminals become celebrities? Is there something exciting about a clever heist?

History's most celebrated heists share a common quality: they expose the gap between institutional confidence in security systems and the ingenuity of those determined to defeat them.

  • The Antwerp Diamond Heist (2003): Thieves bypassed what was described as the world's most secure vault — 123 out of 160 safety deposit boxes were emptied over a weekend. Total losses exceeded $100 million. Most of the loot was never recovered.
  • Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum (1990): Two men dressed as police officers stole 13 works of art worth an estimated $500 million. It remains the largest property theft in US history — no arrests have ever been made.

Discussion: Why do you think some stolen artworks are never sold? What happens to them?

High-profile heists function as cultural texts — they expose contradictions in how societies assign value, protect property, and construct narratives of criminality. The romanticisation of the "gentleman thief" in popular culture reflects an ambivalence about the legitimacy of existing property arrangements.

  • The Baker Street vault robbery (1971) exemplifies institutional failure and state concealment: tunnelling from a rented shop, thieves breached Lloyd's Bank over a bank holiday weekend — and the story was suppressed by a D-notice for decades, suggesting the stolen contents implicated public figures
  • Organised crime heists are distinguished from opportunistic theft by their operational complexity, division of labour, and the sophisticated laundering infrastructure required to monetise the proceeds

Discussion: Should stolen cultural property — art, antiquities — be treated differently from stolen money under criminal law?

Key Takeaways & Discussion

Let's review what we learned today:

  • There are many types of thieves: pickpockets, muggers, burglars, and shoplifters
  • Use rob with a person or a place: "rob the bank", "rob someone"
  • Use steal with an object: "steal money", "steal a car"
  • Thieves use tools like lockpicks and work with accomplices

Final discussion:

  • Have you or anyone you know ever been robbed? What happened?
  • What is the best way to protect yourself from thieves?

Core takeaways from today's lesson:

  • Criminal vocabulary is richly specialised — each type of theft has its own term reflecting the method, target, and level of sophistication
  • Rob targets a person or place; steal targets an object — a distinction mirrored in criminal law (robbery vs theft)
  • Famous heists reveal systemic vulnerabilities: the most elaborate security systems can be defeated by the most creative criminals
  • The criminal economy depends on a chain — thief → fence → buyer — disrupting any link disrupts the whole operation

Final discussion:

  • Is there a moral difference between stealing from a large corporation and stealing from an individual?
  • Do you think tougher sentences actually reduce crime?

Salient conclusions:

  • Criminal typology reflects the socioeconomic conditions of its time — theft vocabulary is a window into how societies organise and protect value
  • The rob/steal distinction maps onto a fundamental legal distinction between robbery (aggravated, victim-present) and theft (non-confrontational larceny)
  • Recidivism data consistently challenge purely punitive approaches — rehabilitation, employment support, and housing stability show stronger long-term results for reducing reoffending
  • The romanticisation of heists in popular culture raises questions about the social construction of criminality and the legitimacy of property relations

Final discussion:

  • Is white-collar crime treated proportionately to the harm it causes?
  • Does criminalising poverty through harsh sentencing for petty theft reflect a failure of social policy?
