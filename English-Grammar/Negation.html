Welcome: Negation in English!

Negation means making a sentence negative — saying something is not true, does not happen, or does not exist. English has four main ways to do this!

NO + noun  →  "I have no money."
NOT + adjective/verb (with be/modal)  →  "She is not ready."
DON'T / DOESN'T + base verb  →  "He doesn't smoke."
HAVEN'T / HASN'T + past participle  →  "I haven't eaten yet."
NO & NOT:

• Used before nouns: "no money"
• Used with "to be": "is not"
• Used with modal verbs: "cannot", "will not"
DON'T / DOESN'T & HAVEN'T / HASN'T:

• Present simple: "I don't know"
• He/she/it: "she doesn't work"
• Present perfect: "I haven't seen"
Key Idea!

The right negative word depends on your verb type. Ask: Is this a noun, a form of "be," a present simple verb, or a present perfect verb?

"No" — Before Nouns

Use NO directly before a noun to say something does not exist or is unavailable

Rule 1 — Singular nouns: "no + noun"

• There is no problem.   • I have no time right now.   • There was no answer.

Rule 2 — Plural nouns: "no + noun"

• There are no seats available.

• We have no classes on Friday.

• There are no buses after midnight.

Rule 3 — Uncountable nouns: "no + noun"

• There is no milk in the fridge.

• He has no experience in this field.

• There is no electricity in the building.

Useful shortcut!

"NO + noun" means the same as "NOT ANY + noun" — "I have no money" = "I don't have any money." Both are correct, but "no" sounds more direct and formal.

"Not" — With "To Be" and Modal Verbs

Use NOT after the verb "to be" and after modal verbs (can, will, should, must…)

Rule 1 — "To be" + not

• I am not tired.   • She is not (isn't) here.   • They are not (aren't) ready.

• He was not (wasn't) at the meeting.   • We were not (weren't) informed.

Rule 2 — Modal verbs + not

• I cannot (can't) drive.

• You should not (shouldn't) smoke here.

• We will not (won't) be late.   • He must not (mustn't) leave.

Rule 3 — "Not" before an infinitive

• Try not to be late.

• I decided not to go.

• It is important not to panic.

Important!

"NOT" cannot stand alone with a regular verb. You need an auxiliary: "I not like it" ✗ → "I do not like it" ✓ — This is where don't/doesn't comes in!

"Don't / Doesn't" — Present Simple Negative

Use DON'T or DOESN'T + the base form of the verb for present simple negatives

This is the most common negation in everyday English. The verb after don't/doesn't does NOT change — it stays in the base form.

I / You / We / They → "don't" (do not)

• I don't like coffee.   • You don't need a jacket today.

• We don't work on Sundays.   • They don't understand.

He / She / It → "doesn't" (does not)

• She doesn't speak French.

• He doesn't want to come.

• The shop doesn't open until 9.   • It doesn't matter.

Common mistake!

After don't/doesn't, the verb stays in its BASE FORM — never add -s! "She doesn't works" ✗ → "She doesn't work" ✓

"Haven't / Hasn't" — Present Perfect Negative

Use HAVEN'T or HASN'T + past participle to say something has not happened up to now

The present perfect connects the past to the present. Use the negative to say something did not happen in a period leading up to now.

I / You / We / They → "haven't" (have not)

• I haven't seen that film yet.

• We haven't eaten lunch.   • They haven't arrived.

• You haven't told me the answer.

He / She / It → "hasn't" (has not)

• She hasn't called back yet.

• He hasn't finished his work.

• The package hasn't arrived.   • My phone hasn't charged.

Common time expressions with present perfect negative:

yet (at the end)  |  still (before haven't)  |  ever / never  |  so far — Example: "I haven't finished yet." / "She still hasn't replied."

Side-by-Side Comparison

Form Used with Example
No Directly before a noun "There is no time."
Not After "to be" / after a modal verb "She is not ready." / "You must not leave."
Don't I / You / We / They + base verb (present simple) "I don't know."
Doesn't He / She / It + base verb (present simple) "She doesn't work here."
Haven't I / You / We / They + past participle "We haven't decided yet."
Hasn't He / She / It + past participle "He hasn't arrived."
Quick Decision Guide!

Noun only? → No.   "To be" or modal? → Not.   Present simple? → Don't / Doesn't.   Present perfect? → Haven't / Hasn't.

Common Errors & How to Fix Them

Error 1 — Using "not" without an auxiliary verb:

✗ I not like this food.

✓ I don't like this food.

Error 2 — Using "don't/doesn't" for the present perfect:

✗ I don't seen him today.

✓ I haven't seen him today.

Error 3 — Adding -s after "doesn't":

✗ She doesn't works here.

✓ She doesn't work here.

Error 4 — Using "no" instead of "not" with verbs:

✗ I no like it. / I am no tired.

✓ I don't like it. / I am not tired.

Always ask:

What TYPE of word comes next — a noun (→ no), a form of be/modal (→ not), a present simple verb (→ don't/doesn't), or a past participle (→ haven't/hasn't)?

"No" and "Not" — Example Sentences

NO — directly before a noun

• There is no coffee left. The pot is empty.

→ "No" negates the noun "coffee" — nothing is there.

• I have no idea what you mean.

→ "No" before the noun "idea" — a very common fixed expression.

• There are no seats on the 9 o'clock train.

→ "No" before the plural noun "seats."

NOT — with "to be" and modal verbs

• The café is not open on Mondays.

→ "Not" after the verb "is" (to be).

• You should not (shouldn't) use your phone in class.

→ "Not" after the modal verb "should."

• I decided not to accept the offer.

→ "Not" before an infinitive to negate it.

"Don't / Doesn't" — Example Sentences

DON'T / DOESN'T — present simple negative

• I don't drink alcohol. I prefer water.

→ "I" → "don't" + base verb "drink."

• She doesn't live in the city centre. She lives in the suburbs.

→ "She" → "doesn't" + base verb "live" (no -s!).

• My brother doesn't eat meat — he's been vegetarian for years.

→ "My brother" = he → "doesn't" + base verb "eat."

• We don't usually go out on Tuesday evenings.

→ "We" → "don't" + base verb "go."

• The restaurant doesn't accept cash. Only card payments.

→ "The restaurant" = it → "doesn't" + base verb "accept."

• They don't know anyone at the party. It's awkward.

→ "They" → "don't" + base verb "know."

"Haven't / Hasn't" — Example Sentences

HAVEN'T / HASN'T — present perfect negative

• I haven't spoken to Maria since Monday.

→ "I" → "haven't" + past participle "spoken." The situation is still relevant now.

• She hasn't replied to my email yet.

→ "She" → "hasn't" + past participle "replied." "Yet" at the end = still waiting.

• We haven't decided where to go for dinner.

→ "We" → "haven't" + past participle "decided."

• He hasn't been to the doctor in over two years.

→ "He" → "hasn't" + past participle "been."

• They haven't signed the contract yet.

→ "They" → "haven't" + past participle "signed." Still pending.

• My boss hasn't seen the report. I'll send it now.

→ "My boss" = he/she → "hasn't" + past participle "seen."

Reading: David's First Week

David has just started a new job in the city. He called his sister to complain. "It's been a difficult week," he said. "I don't know anyone yet, and I haven't had time to find a good café near the office. There are no good restaurants on my street — only fast food places."

His sister asked about his boss. "Is she nice?" David sighed. "She is not unkind, but she doesn't smile very much. She hasn't spoken to me directly yet — she always sends emails. And I haven't understood half of them. There is no dictionary at my desk!"

His sister asked about his flat. "It's OK," he said. "But I haven't unpacked all my boxes yet. There are no curtains in my bedroom, so I wake up at 5 a.m. every morning. And I don't have a washing machine — I have no idea where the nearest laundromat is."

"Don't worry," his sister said. "It's only been a week. You haven't had time to settle in yet. It will get better!"

Reading Analysis

Negation forms found in the text:

NO & NOT (blue) — negating nouns and "to be":

• "no good restaurants" — "no" before a plural noun

• "She is not unkind" — "not" after "to be"

• "no dictionary" — "no" before a singular noun

• "no curtains" — "no" before a plural noun

• "no idea" — "no" before a noun (common expression)

DON'T / DOESN'T & HAVEN'T / HASN'T (green):

• "I don't know anyone" — present simple, subject "I"

• "she doesn't smile" — present simple, subject "she"

• "I haven't had time" — present perfect, subject "I"

• "She hasn't spoken" — present perfect, subject "she"

• "Don't worry" — imperative negative (= do not)

Notice the pattern:

• Before a noun (no article needed) → "no"

• After "is / are / was" → "not"

• Everyday habits and facts → "don't / doesn't"

• Things that haven't happened up to now → "haven't / hasn't"
