Welcome: "There is/are" vs "Have"!

Both THERE IS / ARE and HAVE / HAS describe the world around us — but they are used very differently!

THERE IS / ARE = something exists  |  describing a place or situation

HAVE / HAS = someone owns or possesses something
Use "THERE IS / ARE" to:

• say something exists in a place
• describe what is in a room, city, or building
• introduce something for the first time
• talk about quantity in a location
Use "HAVE / HAS" to:

• express ownership or possession
• say what a person owns
• describe features of an organisation
• talk about what belongs to a subject
Key Idea!

"There is/are" talks about existence — something is present somewhere. "Have/has" talks about possession — a person or organisation owns something.

"There is / There are" — Existence

Use THERE IS / THERE ARE to say that something exists in a place or situation

Rule 1 — Singular: "There is" (or "There's")

• There is a supermarket near my house.

• There is a problem with the computer.

• There's only one chair in the room.

Rule 2 — Plural: "There are"

• There are many people in my office.

• There are three parks in this neighbourhood.

• There are no seats on the bus.

Rule 3 — Uncountable nouns use "There is"

• There is some milk in the fridge.

• There is a lot of traffic today.

• There is no sugar left.

Remember!

The subject comes after the verb: "There is a dog." — "a dog" is the subject. The verb agrees with this subject, not with "there."

"Have / Has" — Possession

Use HAVE / HAS when a person or organisation owns or possesses something

The subject comes first and must be capable of owning something — usually a person, group, or organisation.

I / You / We / They → "have"

• I have a new laptop.

• We have a meeting at 3 o'clock.

• They have two children.

He / She / It → "has"

• She has a beautiful apartment.

• The company has offices in five countries.

• My brother has a dog and a cat.

Key Point!

With "have/has," the subject is the owner. The subject actively possesses the thing. This is very different from "there is/are," where we simply announce that something exists.

The Key Difference

Describing a place or scene → use THERE IS / ARE

Describing ownership → use HAVE / HAS

✓ There are many people in my office. (= people exist in the office)

✗ My office has many people. (= an office cannot own people!)

✓ There are twenty students in the classroom. (= students exist there)

✗ The classroom has twenty students. (= a classroom cannot own students)

✓ There is a swimming pool in our school. (= the pool exists in that location)

✓ Our school has a swimming pool. (= also correct — the school, as an organisation, can own a pool)

The Golden Rule!

A place (room, city, street) can describe what exists in it with "there is/are." Only people and organisations can own things with "have/has." When describing people or living things in a location, always use "there is/are."

Side-by-Side Comparison

Feature "There is / are" "Have / Has"
Announces existence ✓ Yes ✗ No
Expresses ownership ✗ No ✓ Yes
People in a place ✓ "There are 5 people here." ✗ A place cannot own people
Organisation owns something ✓ "There is a gym in the hotel." ✓ "The hotel has a gym."
Person owns something ✗ No ✓ "She has a car."
Uncountable nouns ✓ "There is some water." ✓ "I have some water."
Negative "There isn't / There aren't" "don't have / doesn't have"
Quick Memory Trick!

"There is/are" = existence. "Have/has" = ownership. Ask: "Am I saying something exists, or am I saying someone owns it?"

Common Errors & How to Fix Them

Error 1 — Using "have/has" for people in a place:

✗ My office has many people today.

✓ There are many people in my office today.

Error 2 — Using "there is/are" for personal possession:

✗ There is a car to me. / There is a car for me.

✓ I have a car.

Error 3 — Wrong agreement with "there are":

✗ There is many restaurants in this city.

✓ There are many restaurants in this city.

Error 4 — Confusing a room with an owner:

✗ The waiting room has fifteen patients right now.

✓ There are fifteen patients in the waiting room right now.

Always ask:

Is this about existence (something is present somewhere) or possession (someone owns something)? That question will almost always give you the right answer.

"There is / There are" — Example Sentences

Existence & Location — "THERE IS / THERE ARE"

• There is a post office on the corner of my street.

→ Announcing the existence of a post office in a specific location.

• There are many people waiting outside the restaurant.

→ Describing how many people exist in a particular place.

• There is no milk left in the fridge.

→ Saying that milk does not exist in the fridge (uncountable noun).

• There are three bedrooms in our apartment.

→ Describing what rooms exist in the apartment.

• There is a lot of noise coming from upstairs.

→ Noting the existence of noise in a place.

• There are no seats available on the 8 o'clock train.

→ Saying that seats do not exist (are not available) on that train.

"Have / Has" — Example Sentences

Possession & Ownership — "HAVE / HAS"

• I have a meeting at 10 o'clock this morning.

→ The speaker personally has/owns a commitment.

• She has a beautiful house in the countryside.

→ The house belongs to her — she owns it.

• The hotel has a rooftop pool and a gym.

→ The hotel (as an organisation) owns these facilities.

• My brother has two dogs and a cat.

→ The animals belong to my brother.

• We have a problem — the printer isn't working.

→ The problem belongs to "us" in a figurative sense.

• The company has offices in ten different countries.

→ The company owns / operates those offices.

"There is/are" vs "Have" — Spot the Difference

Compare these pairs — can you see why each choice is correct?

✓ There are thirty employees in this office.

✗ This office has thirty employees. (an office cannot own people)

→ People in a place always use "there are."

✓ There is a gym on the second floor.

✓ The building has a gym on the second floor.

→ Both are correct when talking about facilities — "there is" describes existence; "has" describes the building's features.

✓ I have two sisters.   ✓ There are two sisters in my family.

→ Both correct but different focus: "I have" = my family; "there are" = they exist in my family.

✓ There are a hundred students in the school right now.

✓ The school has over five hundred students enrolled.

→ "There are" = present in the building now; "has" = enrolled (the school owns/manages them as an institution).

Reading: A New Flat

Marco just moved into a new flat in the city. He called his friend to tell him about it. "You have to come and visit!" he said. "It's amazing. There are two big bedrooms and a modern kitchen. There is also a balcony with a great view of the park."

His friend asked about the neighbourhood. "Is it good?" Marco laughed. "It's perfect! There are lots of cafés and restaurants nearby. There is a supermarket right on the corner and a gym two minutes away." He added, "The building has a rooftop terrace and a concierge."

His friend was curious about Marco's things. "Do you have all your furniture yet?" Marco replied, "Not yet. I have a bed and a sofa, but I don't have a dining table. Right now, there are boxes everywhere! There is no space to sit down."

His friend said he would come to help at the weekend. Marco was happy. "Great! I have a coffee machine, so I can make us coffee. And don't worry — there is plenty of room for you to stay."

Color Code:

Blue = "there is / there are"  |  Green = "have / has" — Go to the next slide to analyse each example!

Reading Analysis

"There is/are" and "Have/Has" found in the text:

THERE IS / ARE (blue) — existence:

• "There are two bedrooms" — rooms exist in the flat

• "There is a balcony" — the balcony exists

• "There are lots of cafés" — cafés exist nearby

• "There are boxes everywhere" — boxes exist in the space

• "There is plenty of room" — space exists

HAVE / HAS (green) — possession:

• "The building has a rooftop" — the building (organisation) owns this feature

• "Do you have furniture?" — personal ownership

• "I have a bed and a sofa" — Marco owns them

• "I don't have a dining table" — he doesn't own one

• "I have a coffee machine" — he owns it

Notice the pattern:

• Rooms, spaces, and things in a location → "there is/are"

• Marco personally owns something → "I have"

• The building owns its features → "the building has"
