Necessity & Advice

Today we learn modals: must, have to, should.

We use them to talk about necessity (things that are important to do) and to give advice.

must / have to / should + base verb

Example: You should see a doctor.

"Must" - Strong Obligation

Use "must" for strong obligations, often from the speaker.

Rule:

Use "must" when you think something is very important. It's a personal, strong feeling.

I must finish my homework. (My personal feeling)

You must be quiet in the library. (A strong rule)

We must hurry or we will be late.

Subject + must + base verb

"Have to" - External Obligation

Use "have to" for obligations from outside (rules, laws, other people).

Rule:

"Have to" is for facts, not personal feelings. It's a rule you need to follow.

I have to wear a uniform at work. (It's a rule)

She has to go to the doctor. (The doctor told her to)

Do you have to work on Saturday?

Remember: Use "has to" for he/she/it.

"Should" - Advice

Use "should" to give advice or a suggestion.

Rule:

"Should" means "it's a good idea". It's not a strong obligation.

You look tired. You should rest.

He should study more for the test.

It's a good book. You should read it.

Subject + should + base verb

Negative Forms

Mustn't vs. Don't Have To vs. Shouldn't

Modal Meaning Example
mustn't Prohibition (Don't do it!) You mustn't smoke here.
don't have to No obligation (You can, but it's not necessary) You don't have to come if you're busy.
shouldn't Advice against something You shouldn't eat so much candy.
Important: "Mustn't" and "don't have to" are very different!

Must vs. Have to

Understanding the difference:

Must Have to
Strong personal obligation External rule or obligation
I must stop eating junk food. (My decision) I have to stop eating junk food. (Doctor's order)
You must see this movie! (I think it's great) You have to buy a ticket to see the movie. (Rule)
In many situations, "must" and "have to" can be used with little difference in meaning.

Examples: Must & Have to

Must (Strong personal feeling):

I must call my mom today.
You must try this cake!
We must leave now.
Have to (External rule):

I have to work until 5 PM.
She has to pay her rent.
They have to wear helmets.

Examples: Should & Shouldn't

Should (Advice):

You should drink more water.
He should apologize.
We should go to the beach.
Shouldn't (Advice against):

You shouldn't watch so much TV.
She shouldn't be so rude.
They shouldn't play near the road.

Examples: Questions

A: Do I have to finish this today?
B: Yes, you do. / No, you don't.

A: Should I wear a jacket?
B: Yes, you should. It's cold.

A: What should I do?
B: You should talk to your teacher.

Reading: A Friend's Advice

Maria is talking to her friend, Leo.

Maria: I'm so tired, but I have a big test tomorrow. I must study all night!

Leo: I don't think that's a good idea. You should get some sleep. You can't study if you're too tired.

Maria: But I have to pass this test. It's 50% of my grade!

Leo: I understand, but you shouldn't panic. You've studied a lot already. Maybe you should review your notes for one more hour, and then go to bed.

Maria: Do you really think so? I feel like I must do more.

Leo: Yes. And remember, you don't have to get a perfect score. You just have to pass. You'll do better if you're rested. You mustn't forget to eat breakfast tomorrow morning, either!

Maria: Okay, you're right. Thanks, Leo. I should listen to you more often!
