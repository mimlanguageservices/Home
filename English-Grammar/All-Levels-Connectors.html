Welcome to Connectors!

CONNECTORS are words or phrases that link ideas between sentences and clauses, showing the logical relationship between them.

Idea 1 + connector + Idea 2  →  clear, logical writing
The 5 main types:

• Contrast: however, although
• Addition: furthermore, moreover
• Cause: because, since, as
• Result: therefore, consequently
• Purpose: in order to, so that
Why connectors matter:

• They make your ideas flow smoothly
• They show HOW ideas are related
• They are essential for B1–B2 writing
• They improve speaking fluency
• They are tested in IELTS, FCE & DELF
Key Point!

Different connectors have different grammar rules — some join two clauses, others begin a new sentence. Pay attention to punctuation!

Contrast Connectors

Use these to show an unexpected or opposite idea

Connector Grammar Example
however New sentence. [Connector], She studied hard. However, she failed.
although / even though [Connector] + clause, clause Although she studied, she failed.
despite / in spite of [Connector] + noun/-ing, clause Despite studying hard, she failed.
whereas / while [Connector] + clause, clause He is tall whereas she is short.
nevertheless / yet New sentence. [Connector], It was cold. Nevertheless, they swam.
on the other hand New sentence. [Connector], City life is exciting. On the other hand, it is stressful.
Common Error!

"Despite" and "in spite of" are followed by a NOUN or -ING verb, NOT a full clause. ✓ Despite the rain... ✗ Despite it rained...

Addition Connectors

Use these to add more information or a new point

Formal / Written English

• Furthermore, the results showed a clear improvement. (= also, and what's more)

• Moreover, the new policy has saved the company money. (= and more importantly)

• In addition, students received extra support sessions. (= also)

• Besides, we had no time to prepare. (= also / and anyway)

Formal structures

• Not only does she speak French, but she also speaks Japanese.

• As well as working full-time, he studies at night.

• The hotel was expensive. Furthermore, the service was poor.

• She is intelligent. Moreover, she is extremely hard-working.

• Not only was the film long, but it was also boring.

Register Note!

"Furthermore" and "moreover" are formal. In everyday speech, use "also", "and", or "plus". Save furthermore/moreover for essays and reports.

Cause & Effect Connectors

Cause — explaining WHY something happened

• He stayed home because he was ill. (+ full clause)

• Since she was tired, she went to bed early. (+ full clause, more formal)

• As it was raining, we cancelled the trip. (+ full clause, formal)

• They cancelled the event due to bad weather. (+ noun phrase)

• The match was postponed owing to the storm. (+ noun phrase, formal)

Effect — explaining WHAT happened as a result

• She missed the bus; therefore, she was late. (= so, as a result)

• The road was icy. Consequently, several accidents occurred. (formal)

• He didn't study. As a result, he failed the exam.

• It rained all day, so we stayed inside. (informal)

Key Difference!

"Because" introduces cause. "Therefore" / "so" introduce effect. "Because it rained, we stayed in." = "It rained, so we stayed in."

Purpose Connectors

Use these to explain the GOAL or REASON for an action

Connector Followed by Example
to / in order to infinitive verb She left early to catch the train.
in order to (formal) infinitive verb He trained hard in order to win.
so that subject + verb She spoke slowly so that we understood.
so as to (formal) infinitive verb He whispered so as not to wake her.
for noun / -ing I use this app for learning English.

Negative purpose: "so as not to" / "in order not to"

• She left quietly so as not to disturb anyone.

• He arrived early in order not to miss his flight.

Note!

"To" is the most common and neutral. "In order to" is more formal. "So that" is used when the subject of the two clauses is different or when you use "could/would/can/will".

Time & Sequence Connectors

Simultaneous actions:

• when — When I arrived, she left.
• while / as — While he slept, she worked.
• as soon as — I'll call you as soon as I land.
• the moment (that) — The moment I saw him, I knew.
Before / After:

• before — Eat breakfast before you leave.
• after — After finishing, she relaxed.
• once — Once you try it, you'll love it.
• by the time — By the time he arrived, it was over.
Duration & Limit:

• until / till — Wait until I'm ready.
• since — I've lived here since 2010.
• for — She studied for three hours.
• during — It rained during the night.
Sequence in writing:

• First / Firstly — First, boil the water.
• Then / Next — Then, add the pasta.
• After that — After that, stir well.
• Finally / Lastly — Finally, serve hot.

Contrast & Addition — Word Lists

Contrast — joining clauses:

• although / even though
• while / whereas
• though (informal)

Although it was cold, he wore shorts.
Contrast — new sentence:

• However,
• Nevertheless,
• On the other hand,
• Yet, / Still,

It was cold. However, he wore shorts.
Contrast — before noun/-ing:

• despite
• in spite of

Despite the cold, he wore shorts.
In spite of feeling ill, she went out.
Addition — formal writing:

• Furthermore,
• Moreover,
• In addition,
• Besides,
• Not only... but also
• As well as

Cause, Effect & Purpose — Word Lists

Cause (+ clause):

• because (neutral)
• since (formal/known)
• as (formal/written)

Since you are here, let's start.
Cause (+ noun phrase):

• due to
• owing to (formal)
• because of
• as a result of

Due to the rain, we stayed in.
Effect / Result:

• therefore (formal)
• consequently (formal)
• as a result
• so (informal)
• thus (very formal)

He was late; therefore, he missed it.
Purpose:

• to / in order to
• so that
• so as to (formal)
• for + noun/-ing

She called so that he could prepare.

Connectors in Sentences

Can you identify the type of connector?

• She studied all night; nevertheless, she didn't pass the exam.

→ Contrast: an unexpected result despite the effort

• As it was getting late, they decided to leave the party early.

→ Cause: explains why they left (formal/written style)

• She works part-time in order to spend more time with her children.

→ Purpose: explains the goal of her action

• The project was delayed due to a lack of funding. Consequently, the launch was postponed.

→ Cause (due to) + Effect (consequently): a chain of events

• Not only did he win the race, but he also broke the national record.

→ Addition: emphasises two impressive achievements (note: inverted subject-verb after "not only")

• Whereas city life offers many opportunities, rural life is quieter and less stressful.

→ Contrast: directly comparing two different situations

Reading: A Change of Direction

Lucas had always dreamed of becoming a professional musician. However, his parents were not convinced it was a reliable career. Although he had real talent, they encouraged him to study business instead.

Since he wanted to keep the peace, Lucas enrolled in a business degree. Nevertheless, he continued playing guitar every evening in order to keep his dream alive. As a result, he became incredibly skilled over the years.

After graduating, he worked at a company for three years. Meanwhile, he uploaded videos of his music online so that people could discover him. Due to his consistency and talent, one of his videos went viral.

He was soon offered a record deal. Consequently, he left his job and followed his passion. Not only did he release a successful album, but he also began touring internationally. Despite the initial doubts, Lucas proved that following your dreams can be the right choice.

Reading Analysis

Connectors found in the text:

• However, — contrast (new sentence)

• Although — contrast (clause connector)

• Since — cause (formal)

• Nevertheless, — contrast (new sentence)

• in order to — purpose

• As a result, — effect/result

• After — time (sequence)

• Meanwhile, — time (simultaneous)

• so that — purpose

• Due to — cause (+ noun phrase)

• Consequently, — effect/result

• Not only... but also — addition

• Despite — contrast (+ noun phrase)

Notice:

• "Since" = cause here (not time), because it means "because"

• "Due to" is followed by a noun phrase: "his consistency and talent"

• "Not only did he..." uses inverted word order (question form), which is required in formal writing
