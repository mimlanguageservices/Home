Welcome to Compound Adjectives!

A COMPOUND ADJECTIVE is two or more words joined together to modify a noun.

word + word  →  compound adjective + noun
Before a noun:

• a well-known actor
• a five-star hotel
• a hard-working student
• a time-consuming task
Four main patterns:

• Adv + past participle: well-known
• Adj + present participle: hard-working
• Noun + participle: time-consuming
• Number + noun: five-star
Key Point!

Compound adjectives are almost always hyphenated when placed before a noun. See Slide 6 for the full hyphen rules.

Pattern 1: Adverb + Past Participle

Structure: adverb + past participle

Adverb Past Participle Compound Adjective Meaning
well known well-known famous, recognised by many
well dressed well-dressed wearing smart, elegant clothes
well paid well-paid receiving a good salary
badly written badly-written poor in quality of writing
newly built newly-built recently constructed
widely used widely-used used by many people

• He is a well-known politician. (= He is known by many people.)

• She has a well-paid job in finance. (= Her salary is very good.)

• It was a badly-written report. (= The report was written poorly.)

Note!

"well" is the most common adverb in this pattern. Other common ones: badly, newly, poorly, deeply, broadly, widely.

Pattern 2: Adjective + Present Participle

Structure: adjective + present participle (-ing)

These compounds describe an ongoing quality or characteristic.

Personality & Appearance:

• good-looking (attractive)
• easy-going (relaxed, calm)
• hard-working (diligent)
• high-achieving (very successful)
Qualities & Features:

• long-lasting (lasts a long time)
• fast-moving (moves quickly)
• quick-thinking (reacts fast)
• free-standing (stands alone)

• She is a hard-working student. (= She works hard.)

• My boss is very easy-going. He never gets angry.

• This is a long-lasting solution to the problem.

How to understand the meaning:

• hard-working → works hard

• good-looking → looks good

• fast-moving → moves fast

The adjective tells us how or in what way the action happens.

Pattern 3: Noun + Participle

Sub-type A: Noun + Past Participle

The noun is the means or agent of the action.

• hand-made (made by hand) — hand-made furniture

• home-grown (grown at home) — home-grown vegetables

• sun-dried (dried by the sun) — sun-dried tomatoes

• heart-broken (broken by heartache) — a heart-broken teenager

• award-winning (winning awards) — an award-winning film

Sub-type B: Noun + Present Participle

The noun is the object of the action.

• time-consuming (time is consumed) — a time-consuming process

• mouth-watering (makes your mouth water) — a mouth-watering dish

• record-breaking (breaks a record) — a record-breaking performance

• mind-blowing (blows your mind) — a mind-blowing discovery

Quick Check!

hand-MADE → your hand does the making. time-CONSUMING → time is what gets consumed. Ask: is the noun doing the action, or receiving it?

Pattern 4: Number / Adjective + Noun

Number + Noun — the noun is always singular!

Number Noun (singular) Example phrase
five star a five-star hotel
three year a three-year-old child
two hour a two-hour delay
ten minute a ten-minute walk
Common Error!

a five-star hotel ✓  |  a five-stars hotel ✗   — the noun is always singular in this pattern.

Adjective + Noun

• full-time / part-time — She has a full-time job.

• last-minute — He always makes last-minute decisions.

• old-fashioned — She loves old-fashioned music.

• short-sighted / open-minded — He is very open-minded about new ideas.

• long-distance — They are in a long-distance relationship.

Hyphen Rules

Rule 1 — Before a noun: USE a hyphen ✓

• She is a well-known actress.

• It was a time-consuming project.

• He made a last-minute decision.

Rule 2 — After "be" (predicative): usually NO hyphen ✓

• The actress is well known. (no hyphen)

• The project was very time consuming. (no hyphen)

Rule 3 — Adverbs ending in -ly: NEVER a hyphen ✓

• a highly respected professor ✓  (NOT: highly-respected ✗)

• a beautifully designed building ✓  (NOT: beautifully-designed ✗)

Rule 4 — Number compounds: ALWAYS a hyphen ✓

• a five-star hotel  /  The hotel is five-star.

• a three-year-old child  /  My child is three years old.

Why hyphens matter!

Without a hyphen, meaning can change: "a small business owner" = a business owner who is small. "a small-business owner" = someone who owns a small business!

Word Lists: Patterns 1 & 2

Adverb + Past Participle:

• well-known, well-dressed
• well-behaved, well-paid
• well-spoken, well-read
• well-travelled, well-organised
• badly-written, poorly-lit
• newly-built, widely-used
Adjective + Present Participle:

• good-looking, hard-working
• easy-going, fast-moving
• long-lasting, high-achieving
• quick-thinking, free-standing
• eye-catching, far-reaching
• slow-moving, smooth-talking
Opposite pairs:

• well-paid ↔ badly-paid
• full-time ↔ part-time
• open-minded ↔ narrow-minded
• short-sighted ↔ long-sighted
• hard-working ↔ easy-going
• well-known ↔ little-known
Common "well-" compounds:

• well-known (famous)
• well-off (wealthy)
• well-built (strong body)
• well-educated (highly educated)
• well-meaning (with good intentions)
• well-deserved (truly earned)

Word Lists: Patterns 3 & 4

Noun + Past Participle:

• hand-made, home-grown
• sun-dried, wind-powered
• heart-broken, custom-built
• award-winning, battle-tested

Noun + Present Participle:

• time-consuming, money-saving

• record-breaking, mind-blowing

• energy-saving, back-breaking
Number + Noun (singular):

• five-star hotel

Adjective + Noun:

• full-time / part-time

Compound Adjectives in Sentences

Can you identify the pattern?

• He is a well-known chef who has appeared on television many times.

→ Pattern 1: adverb (well) + past participle (known)

• The training course is very time-consuming but absolutely worth it.

→ Pattern 3B: noun (time) + present participle (consuming)

• She has a part-time job at the local café on weekends.

→ Pattern 4: adjective (part) + noun (time)

• The market sells hand-made jewellery and home-grown produce.

→ Pattern 3A: noun + past participle (both)

• We stayed in a five-star hotel with a breath-taking view of the ocean.

→ Pattern 4 (five-star): number + noun  |  Pattern 3B (breath-taking): noun + present participle

• He is easy-going and good-looking — no wonder he has so many friends!

→ Pattern 2: adjective + present participle (both)

Reading: A World-Famous Chef

Maria Fernandez is a well-known chef from Mexico City. She grew up in a small, old-fashioned kitchen where her grandmother cooked mouth-watering dishes using hand-picked herbs from the garden.

As a teenager, Maria was hard-working and open-minded. She read every cookbook she could find and practised for hours each day. By her early twenties, she had become a highly respected culinary student in Paris.

After a back-breaking five-year training programme, she returned to Mexico and opened her own restaurant. The menu featured sun-dried tomatoes, home-grown vegetables, and award-winning recipes passed down through generations.

Today, Maria runs a full-time cooking show and a part-time school for young chefs. She is easy-going with her students but has sky-high standards in the kitchen. Her restaurant is so popular that last-minute reservations are almost impossible to get.

Reading Analysis

Compound adjectives found in the text:

• well-known — adverb + past participle

• old-fashioned — adjective + past participle

• mouth-watering — noun + present participle

• hand-picked — noun + past participle

• hard-working — adjective + present participle

• open-minded — adjective + past participle

• back-breaking — noun + present participle

• five-year — number + noun

• sun-dried — noun + past participle

• home-grown — noun + past participle

• award-winning — noun + present participle

• full-time — adjective + noun

• part-time — adjective + noun

• easy-going — adjective + present participle

• sky-high — noun + adjective

• last-minute — adjective + noun

Notice:

• "highly respected" has NO hyphen because "highly" ends in -ly

• All 16 compounds appear before a noun, so they all use hyphens

• Compound adjectives make descriptions more vivid and precise
