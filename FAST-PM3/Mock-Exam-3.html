Literary Text · Short Story
The Long Way Home
An original short story for FAST PM3 practice

DeShawn found the bicycle wedged behind Mr. Alvarez's garage on a Saturday in October, its frame orange with rust, one wheel bent into something closer to an oval than a circle. He almost walked past it. Almost.

He knew whose block needed a bicycle repaired, though the bicycle wasn't for him. Three weeks earlier, the city had cut the number 14 bus route — the only one that stopped near their street — citing low ridership and a shrinking budget. For most people on the block, this was an inconvenience. For Ms. Odessa, who was seventy-eight and walked with a cane after her hip surgery, it meant a forty-minute walk to the grocery store on Fifth, each way, twice a week, in whatever weather decided to show up.

"That's not your problem to fix, baby," his mother said when he mentioned it, not unkindly, drying a dish at the sink. "You've got school. You've got basketball. Let the city figure out the bus."

DeShawn didn't argue. He just started spending his Saturday mornings in the garage with a wrench that was too big for half the bolts and a video tutorial paused every four seconds. Fixing the wheel took three weekends — he bent it back by degrees, the way you might slowly convince someone of something they didn't want to believe, a little more each time, never all at once.

Ms. Odessa noticed him working before he was ready to explain. She came out onto her porch one Saturday holding two glasses of lemonade and simply set one down on the step near him, without a word about what he was doing or why. She did this every Saturday after that. Neither of them mentioned the bicycle directly for weeks.

The cost of all this was small but real. DeShawn missed two Saturday pickup games at the courts on Bellamy, and Marcus texted him twice asking where he'd been, and DeShawn didn't know how to explain — not because it was embarrassing, exactly, but because it was hard to make "I'm fixing an old bike for my elderly neighbor" sound like something a fifteen-year-old was supposed to care about. So he just said he was busy.

By the second week of November, the bicycle worked. Really worked — chain oiled, brakes gripping, wheel spinning true. DeShawn wheeled it over to Ms. Odessa's porch on a Saturday morning and didn't say much, just: "Basket's a little small, but it should hold your groceries."

Ms. Odessa looked at the bicycle for a long moment, then at DeShawn, then back at the bicycle. "I haven't ridden a bicycle since 1987," she said finally. "You think these knees remember how?"

"Only one way to find out," DeShawn said.

She didn't ride it that day. But the following Tuesday, DeShawn saw her from his window, wobbling a little at first down the sidewalk, cane strapped to the frame, moving toward Fifth Street at a pace that would have taken her forty minutes on foot and now took her twelve. She didn't wave. She didn't need to. Some kindnesses are loud enough to be understood in silence.

Life Without Light: Discovery at the Ocean Floor
Adapted from marine science reporting · FAST PM3 practice

In 1977, a team of geologists aboard the submersible Alvin descended nearly 8,000 feet to the ocean floor near the Galápagos Islands, expecting to study underwater volcanic activity. They did not expect to find life. Yet clustered around towering plumes of mineral-rich, superheated water — later named hydrothermal vents — the scientists observed dense colonies of organisms unlike anything previously catalogued: giant tube worms over six feet long, ghostly white crabs, and clams the size of dinner plates, thriving in what should have been one of the most hostile environments on Earth.

The discovery upended a foundational assumption of biology. Virtually all known ecosystems on Earth were understood to depend, directly or indirectly, on photosynthesis — the process by which plants and algae convert sunlight into usable energy. At depths of 8,000 feet, sunlight does not penetrate; the vent ecosystems exist in permanent, absolute darkness. If not sunlight, what powered this thriving community?

The answer, biologists soon discovered, was chemosynthesis. Bacteria living inside the tissues of the tube worms and other vent organisms convert hydrogen sulfide — a chemical compound spewed from the vents that is toxic to most life — into usable energy through a chemical process entirely independent of sunlight. The tube worms, remarkably, have no mouth, no stomach, and no digestive tract; they rely entirely on their internal bacterial partners to convert chemicals into nutrients, a relationship so complete that neither organism can survive without the other.

This discovery had implications far beyond marine biology. If life could flourish in total darkness, fueled by chemistry rather than sunlight, scientists began to reconsider where else in the universe life might exist. Jupiter's moon Europa and Saturn's moon Enceladus are both believed to harbor vast oceans of liquid water beneath thick ice shells — environments that, like the deep ocean floor, never receive sunlight. NASA has since prioritized both moons for future exploration missions, reasoning that if chemosynthetic life can exist in Earth's darkest depths, similar life might exist in the darkest depths of other worlds.

Hydrothermal vent ecosystems are also remarkably temporary by geological standards. A single vent may remain active for only a few decades before the underlying magma shifts and the flow of mineral-rich water stops, killing the community that depended on it. Yet within a few years, an entirely new community typically colonizes a newly formed vent nearby — evidence that vent organisms, despite their alien appearance, have evolved efficient strategies for dispersal and rapid colonization across the otherwise empty seafloor.

Fewer people have visited hydrothermal vents than have walked on the moon. Yet the ecosystems clustered around them have quietly reshaped one of biology's oldest assumptions: that all life, everywhere, depends on the sun.

Silence and Sound: Two Views
FAST PM3 practice · Paired texts

Passage 1 — "The Vanishing Quiet" (Informational)

Adapted from environmental and health science reporting

In 2011, the World Health Organization estimated that traffic noise alone was responsible for the loss of more than one million healthy life years annually across Western Europe — a burden the WHO ranked second only to air pollution among environmental health threats. Yet unlike air pollution, chronic noise exposure remains largely invisible in public conversation, treated as an unavoidable feature of modern life rather than a measurable danger.

The health effects of constant noise exposure are well documented. Even at levels too low to cause hearing damage, chronic exposure to environmental noise elevates cortisol and other stress hormones, disrupts sleep architecture, and has been linked to increased rates of hypertension and cardiovascular disease. Researchers studying residents near airports and highways have found measurable increases in heart attack risk correlated with proximity to consistent noise sources — effects that persist even among people who report having "gotten used to" the sound.

Wildlife faces its own version of this crisis. Many songbird species have been documented singing at higher pitches and greater volumes in urban environments, straining to be heard over traffic — a costly adaptation that can reduce the range and complexity of their songs, which are essential for attracting mates. Whales, which rely on low-frequency calls to communicate across vast stretches of ocean, must now compete with the noise of commercial shipping traffic; some researchers estimate that the effective range of whale communication has been reduced by as much as ninety percent in heavily trafficked waters compared to a century ago.

In response, a small but growing movement has emerged to identify and protect the world's remaining quiet places. The nonprofit Quiet Parks International has begun certifying locations — from remote forests to sections of open ocean — that meet strict criteria for the absence of persistent human-made noise. Acoustic ecologist Gordon Hempton, who helped pioneer this effort, has argued that natural silence is not merely the absence of sound but a resource in its own right, one that is disappearing as quickly as any endangered species.

Unlike a forest or a wetland, silence cannot be replanted once it is gone; a single flight path, highway, or drilling operation can permanently alter the acoustic character of a landscape that took no human effort to create and, once lost, may take generations of policy change to restore, if it can be restored at all.

Passage 2 — "What the Quiet Holds" (Literary · Poem)

An original poem for FAST PM3 practice

My grandfather used to cut the engine
halfway across the lake
and let the boat drift,
not fishing, not really —
just floating in what he called
the good kind of nothing.

I was young enough to find this strange.
I wanted the radio, the motor's hum,
anything with a shape I could name.

He'd say: listen long enough
and the quiet stops being empty.
You start to hear the water
against the hull, a heron
somewhere deciding whether to trust you,
your own breath, which you forget
you're always making.

Now I drive home each night
past the airport, the interstate,
a hundred small machines
insisting on themselves,
and I understand what he was teaching me
was not about the lake at all —

it was that a person needs
somewhere the world stops shouting
long enough to hear
what they actually think.

Last summer I brought my daughter
to that same lake, cut the engine
the way he taught me,
and watched her face change
the way mine must have,
from waiting for something
to happening to notice everything.
