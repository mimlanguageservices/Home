Literary Text · Short Story
The Weight of Salt
An original short story for FAST PM3 practice

Marisol had learned to read her grandmother's silences the way a sailor reads the sea—by the quality of the stillness, the direction of her gaze, the particular way her hands moved or refused to move. That morning, Abuela Concha sat at the kitchen table without touching her coffee, which meant something heavier than ordinary worry.

"The offer is generous," Marisol said, setting down her own mug. The real estate developer had left three voicemails. The number was more than Marisol would make in five years at the school. "We don't have to decide today."

Her grandmother's fingers traced the grain of the table—the same table that had survived two hurricanes, a kitchen fire, and forty-three years of daily use. It had been refinished so many times that the wood had taken on a dull, moon-colored sheen in the center where plates had rubbed it smooth.

"Your grandfather built this table from the cedar in the backyard," Abuela Concha said. "Not that cedar." She gestured toward the window. "The one the storm took in '92. He saved the wood for two years before he made this."

Marisol sat down across from her. She recognized this kind of talking—not a direct answer, but a path through a thicket toward one. She waited.

"When we first came here," her grandmother continued, "we had almost nothing. Your grandfather worked shrimping boats. I cleaned houses. But this—" she pressed both palms flat on the table "—this was the first thing we made that was ours. Not rented. Not borrowed. Made."

Outside, a car slowed in front of the house and moved on. The developer's representative had driven past twice last week. Marisol noticed her grandmother notice it too—the slight stiffening of the shoulders, the minute lift of her chin.

"The neighborhood is changing," Marisol said carefully. Three of the six houses on the block had already sold.

"Neighborhoods always change," her grandmother said. "People change. What stays—" She picked up her coffee and finally drank. "What stays is what you decide to hold onto."

Marisol looked around the kitchen: the saint's candle on the windowsill, the calendar from the Cuban grocery that had closed in 2019, the smell of salt air that seemed to live in the walls themselves. She thought about the number the developer had offered. She thought about her daughter's tuition. She thought about the look on her grandmother's face when she pressed her hands against that old cedar table, and how it was the same look Marisol had seen only twice before—once at her grandfather's funeral, and once when they had watched a photograph of this house burn in a frame.

She reached across the table and covered her grandmother's hands with her own.

"Tell them no," she said.

Abuela Concha did not smile, exactly. But something in her face became lighter, the way the sky sometimes becomes lighter before you can name the moment the storm has passed.

Rewilding the Everglades
Adapted from environmental science reporting · FAST PM3 practice

For most of the twentieth century, engineers treated South Florida's Everglades as a problem to be solved. Beginning in the 1940s, the United States Army Corps of Engineers drained more than half of the original wetlands, channeling water through 1,700 miles of canals and levees designed to protect farms and cities from flooding. The results seemed, for a time, like a triumph of human ambition over nature. Within decades, however, scientists began to document a cascade of unintended consequences that would take generations to repair.

The Everglades is not, as it is sometimes called, a swamp. It is more accurately described as a slow-moving river of grass—a vast, shallow sheet of freshwater roughly 60 miles wide and 100 miles long, flowing south from Lake Okeechobee to Florida Bay. This unique hydrology supports an extraordinary range of species: more than 350 types of birds, dozens of endangered animals, and plants found nowhere else on Earth. The drainage project of the mid-twentieth century did not simply remove water. It dismantled an entire ecological web.

Among the most visible indicators of damage was the collapse of wading bird populations. In the early 1900s, researchers estimated that more than 2.5 million wading birds nested in the Everglades each year. By 1990, that number had fallen by more than ninety percent. The birds depend on seasonal flooding cycles that concentrate fish in shallow pools—a food source that disappeared as water flow was artificially controlled. Without reliable nesting conditions, colonies that had persisted for thousands of years vanished within a few decades.

In 2000, the United States government approved the Comprehensive Everglades Restoration Plan (CERP), the largest environmental restoration project in American history. With an estimated cost of $10.9 billion and a timeline stretching across decades, CERP aims to restore the natural water flow that sustained the ecosystem before human intervention. Engineers are dismantling some of the old drainage infrastructure and constructing new reservoirs that can store and release water in patterns that mimic natural flooding cycles.

The results, while encouraging, are uneven. Populations of some bird species have begun to recover, and water quality in certain areas has improved measurably. Yet the Everglades also faces pressures that did not exist in the 1940s: rising sea levels, more intense hurricanes, and the expansion of invasive species—particularly the Burmese python, which arrived in South Florida likely as a result of the exotic pet trade and now threatens virtually every native mammal in the region.

Scientists and policy makers increasingly argue that restoration of the Everglades must be understood not as a single goal to be completed, but as an ongoing commitment. "We're not trying to recreate 1900," explained Dr. Evelyn Torres, an ecologist at the University of Florida. "We're trying to build an ecosystem resilient enough to adapt to conditions that haven't happened yet."

Whether that goal is achievable depends in part on political will—a resource, unlike water, that has proven difficult to sustain over the long timeframes that ecological recovery requires. Funding for CERP has frequently fallen short of projections, and some restoration milestones that were meant to be completed years ago remain unfinished. The Everglades, it turns out, is not a problem that can be solved on a human schedule.

Passage 1 — "The Science of Monarch Migration" (Informational)

Adapted from natural science reporting

Each autumn, more than a billion monarch butterflies undertake one of the most remarkable journeys in the animal kingdom, traveling up to 3,000 miles from their summer habitats in the United States and Canada to overwintering sites in the oyamel fir forests of central Mexico. What makes this migration especially astonishing is that no individual butterfly completes the round trip. The monarchs that arrive in Mexico in November are the great-great-grandchildren of the butterflies that left Mexico the previous spring.

Scientists call this a multigenerational migration. The butterflies that travel south in the fall are a distinct generation—sometimes called the "Methuselah generation"—that lives far longer than the summer generations and carries a genetic "memory" for direction that enables them to navigate thousands of miles to a location they have never seen. Researchers believe this navigational ability is linked to a time-compensated sun compass, allowing monarchs to use the position of the sun in combination with an internal circadian clock to maintain a consistent flight direction.

The monarch migration is now considered one of the most endangered biological phenomena on Earth. Habitat loss, the elimination of milkweed (the caterpillar's only food source) through widespread herbicide use, climate change, and illegal logging at overwintering sites have reduced the population dramatically. In the 1990s, some surveys estimated more than one billion monarchs. Recent counts have recorded numbers as low as 35 million—a decline of approximately ninety-seven percent.

Conservation efforts have focused on planting milkweed corridors along migration routes, reducing pesticide use, and protecting the Mexican forests. Some researchers are cautiously optimistic; recent years have shown modest population increases. Others warn that the system may be approaching a tipping point from which recovery is unlikely without dramatic intervention.

Passage 2 — "Flyway" (Literary · Poem)

An original poem for FAST PM3 practice

They have never been here before,
these wings unfolding at the edge of October,
and yet they know the way—
the whole warm south of it,
stored in the flicker of a vein.

What we call instinct, they call
morning. What we call loss
they simply fly through,
indifferent to the grief
of those who watch them go.

My grandmother crossed water once
and never crossed it back.
She said the body remembers
even what the mind has let go—
the way your feet know the angle of a hill
you climbed before you learned to name hills.

October again. The monarchs
move in their amber thousands
and I think: the journey is the only country
some of us were ever given.
