Literary Text · Short Story
The Cartographer's Daughter
An original short story for FAST PM3 practice

Maya had always known her father by his hands — the way they moved over a map with the reverence most people reserved for scripture, tracing coastlines like a man reading a face. Now those hands were still, and the maps remained.

She stood in the doorway of his study, which smelled of India ink and old paper and something else she could not name, something that simply meant him. Her mother had called it "clutter." The real estate agent had called it "opportunity." Maya called it the last room she was not ready to enter.

"We need to decide by Friday," her mother said from the hallway, not unkindly. "The offer won't wait."

Maya stepped inside. The drafting table still held his final project — a topographic map of a mountain range in the Andes that no one had commissioned, that her father had been drawing simply because, he said, some places deserved to exist on paper. Half-finished. The mountains rose in careful brown contour lines on the left side of the sheet; the right side was blank.

She sat in his chair, which was worn to the shape of him. On the shelf above the table, rows of finished maps stood in rolled tubes, each labeled in his precise handwriting. Forty years of work. Places she had never been: river deltas, island chains, cities whose names she could not pronounce. He had told her once that a map was an act of love — that you couldn't draw a place carefully without coming to care for it.

"You understand things by paying attention to them," he had said. She had been twelve, impatient, wanting to go outside. "The map isn't the place. But making it changes you."

Maya picked up his drafting pencil. It was still sharp. She positioned it above the blank half of the Andes sheet and then did not move for a long time.

Her phone buzzed. Her mother again. Maya set the phone face-down.

She thought about the apartment — what selling it would mean. Freedom from the rent, her mother said. A fresh start. She thought about the real estate agent's smile, too wide, too certain. She thought about her father's voice: some places deserve to exist on paper.

And then, without deciding exactly when she had decided, she began to draw.

The first line was uncertain, tentative — a ridge that barely committed to its own direction. But the second line was steadier. By the third, her hand remembered something it had not known it knew: the pressure of the pencil, the angle of the wrist, the way attention and care could become, over an hour, something that looked like knowing.

When her mother appeared in the doorway again, Maya did not hear her at first.

"Maya." Her mother's voice was different now — quieter, caught on something.

Maya looked up. Her mother was looking at the map, at the lines Maya had drawn, and her expression held something that was not grief exactly, and not pride exactly, but lived somewhere between the two, the way some territories resist being named.

"We don't have to decide today," her mother said.

The Hidden Forest
Adapted from environmental science reporting · FAST PM3 practice

For most of human history, forests appeared to be collections of individual organisms competing for sunlight, water, and soil nutrients. Trees that grew taller survived; those that did not were shaded out and died. This was the story scientists told, and for a long time, it seemed to explain what was visible above ground. What it failed to explain was what was happening underground.

In the 1990s, forest ecologist Suzanne Simard began publishing research that would upend decades of conventional thinking about how forests function. Working in the old-growth forests of British Columbia, Simard and her colleagues discovered that the roots of trees are interconnected through vast networks of fungi called mycorrhizae — organisms that form symbiotic relationships with plant roots and can extend for miles through the soil. Through these networks, trees do not simply coexist. They share resources and, in some cases, actively support one another.

Simard's experiments demonstrated that larger, older trees — which she termed "mother trees" — transferred carbon, nitrogen, and water to younger seedlings growing in their shade. When a mother tree was cut down, the seedlings nearby showed measurably reduced growth rates and higher mortality. The fungi connecting them did not simply transport nutrients randomly; they appeared to prioritize flows toward plants under stress.

This discovery challenged the foundational assumption of competitive individualism in plant science. "We used to think the forest was a marketplace," Simard has said. "Now we know it's more like a family." Critics of this framing argue that attributing family-like behavior to fungi and trees is a form of anthropomorphism — projecting human social structures onto biological processes that are simply chemical signals and resource gradients. The debate continues, but few scientists dispute the underlying data: the mycorrhizal network exists, it transfers resources, and it responds dynamically to stress.

The practical implications of this research extend into forestry policy. For decades, the standard practice in commercial forestry was to clear-cut an area and replant a single species — often chosen for fast growth and market value. This approach, researchers now argue, destroys the underground fungal networks that take generations to develop. A newly planted monoculture forest, however dense, lacks the mycorrhizal infrastructure that enables older forests to withstand drought, disease, and insect infestation.

Conservation biologists have used Simard's findings to argue for what they call "network-aware forestry" — harvest practices that preserve old-growth anchor trees and maintain mycorrhizal continuity across logged areas. Some commercial forestry companies have begun piloting these approaches, though widespread adoption has been slow. The economics of old-growth preservation remain in tension with short-term timber profits.

What Simard's work has made increasingly difficult to ignore is a question that sits at the edge of science and philosophy: what do we mean by an individual? If a forest shares resources, responds collectively to stress, and maintains its health through cooperative underground exchange, the boundary between one organism and another becomes harder to draw than the trees above ground make it appear.

Light and Darkness: Two Views
FAST PM3 practice · Paired texts

Passage 1 — "The Vanishing Sky" (Informational)

Adapted from science and environment reporting

A century ago, the Milky Way was visible to nearly everyone on Earth on a clear night. Today, roughly one-third of the global population — including eighty percent of Americans — cannot see it from where they live. The cause is light pollution: the excess artificial light that fills the night sky, washing out the stars that humans have used for navigation, storytelling, and orientation since the beginning of recorded history.

Light pollution is not merely an aesthetic problem. Biologists have documented significant effects on species whose behavior depends on natural light cycles. Sea turtle hatchlings, which instinctively move toward the brightest horizon (historically the moon's reflection on the water), now frequently crawl inland toward streetlights and die before reaching the ocean. Migratory birds navigate by stars and become disoriented by brightly lit skyscrapers, leading to fatal collisions; researchers estimate that one billion birds die annually from building strikes in the United States, with nighttime light a major contributing factor.

The effects on humans are increasingly well-documented as well. Exposure to artificial light at night suppresses the production of melatonin, a hormone that regulates sleep and has been linked to immune function and the prevention of certain cancers. Studies of nurses working night shifts have found elevated rates of breast cancer, a connection researchers attribute in part to chronic melatonin disruption.

Solutions exist and are relatively inexpensive. Shielded light fixtures that direct illumination downward rather than into the sky reduce light pollution dramatically without reducing visibility on the ground. Replacing high-intensity white LEDs with warmer, lower-intensity alternatives mitigates biological harm while cutting energy costs. Dark-sky preserves — zones where outdoor lighting is strictly regulated — have been established in ecologically and astronomically significant areas, and many have documented measurable recoveries in nocturnal wildlife populations within years of implementation.

The International Dark-Sky Association estimates that thirty percent of outdoor lighting in the United States is wasted — directed upward or sideways rather than at its intended target. The energy cost of this waste is approximately $3.3 billion annually. In other words, light pollution is not only damaging ecosystems and human health; it is, quite literally, money thrown at the sky.

Passage 2 — "What We Buried" (Literary · Poem)

An original poem for FAST PM3 practice

Before the lights, my grandmother
could find her way home by Orion's belt —
three stars she called her compass,
her roof when she was lost.

Now I drive home under orange cloud,
the kind that isn't weather,
and the children I teach
have never seen the Milky Way
except in photographs.

They think of it the way they think of whales —
something enormous and elsewhere,
something you watch on a screen.

Last summer we drove four hours
to a place where the sky still worked.
My daughter said: is that real?
And I didn't know whether to laugh
or to understand, finally,
what it is we have buried
under all this borrowed light —

not just the stars,
but the particular dark
that teaches you your own smallness,
and how to bear it.
